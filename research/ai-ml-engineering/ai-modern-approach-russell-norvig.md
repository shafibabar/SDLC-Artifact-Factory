# Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach
**Authors:** Stuart Russell and Peter Norvig
**Domain:** AI/ML Engineering (the canonical comprehensive AI textbook — search, knowledge representation and logic, planning, probabilistic reasoning, classical/statistical machine learning, reinforcement learning, NLP, robotics, and AI safety/ethics)

## Core Concepts

- **This book's core stance is nearly the mirror image of the four prior files in this cluster — a deliberate methodological alternative, not another variant technique.** `ai-engineering-huyen.md`, `designing-ml-systems-huyen.md`, `llm-engineering-handbook-iusztin-labonne.md`, and `build-llm-from-scratch-raschka.md` all assume, correctly for most of this product's roster, that "classify this document" or "extract this entity" is answered by some flavor of learned model — prompted, fine-tuned, or trained from scratch. AIMA's knowledge-representation, logic, and constraint-satisfaction chapters (Ch. 7-9, 18 in the 4th edition) describe a genuinely different family of techniques: a decision computed by explicit, hand-authored rules or constraints whose reasoning is inspectable step by step, with no training data, no confidence score, and no calibration question, because the answer is a deduction from stated premises rather than an inference from a trained function. This is the single most transferable idea in this book for this repo, precisely because this repo's core compliance premise — a SOC 2 auditor asking "why was this flagged" — is the textbook use case deterministic, logic-based reasoning was built to answer, and none of the four prior books in this cluster (all model-centric) can answer it by construction.
- **Rule-based inference (forward/backward chaining, production rule systems) is a concrete, named alternative to the still-unstated "Automated detection" verb book 1 (`ai-engineering-huyen.md`) already flagged.** `skills/data-classification/SKILL.md`'s "Automated PII detection" section (read in full, lines 68-81) states detection *inputs and outputs* — entity type, provisional level, confidence score, threshold — but never the technique. Book 1 proposed the technique be named as one of regex/rule-based, fine-tuned classifier, or LLM prompt. AIMA supplies a fourth, real option for the *semantic* judgments book 1 identified as genuinely needing more than pattern-matching (is this paragraph about health information? is this whole document's tone Confidential vs. Restricted?): an expert-system-style forward-chaining rule base of if-then production rules over already-extracted features, where every fired rule and the fact that triggered it is retained, producing an audit trail *by construction* rather than as an evaluation afterthought — no golden dataset, no calibration step, no span-aggregation policy (Raschka's finding) required, because there is no token-level model underneath to calibrate or aggregate.
- **Constraint Satisfaction Problems (CSPs) give a name to a pattern `data-classification` already implements without naming it.** The skill's propagation formula (`SKILL.md` lines 85-92: `effective_level(asset) = manual_override_level if set, else max(...)`, with the override sitting outside the `max` and escalation-past-override triggering re-review, not silence) is, mechanically, a small, deterministic constraint-satisfaction computation — a fixed set of constraints (the max rule, override precedence, escalation-triggers-review) evaluated over a small set of variables. AIMA's CSP chapter (backtracking search, arc consistency, constraint propagation) is exactly the formal apparatus this repo's own propagation logic is already, informally, an instance of. This matters concretely the moment the propagation rules grow more complex — multiple special categories interacting, or a future cross-asset constraint (e.g., "no two related assets may hold inconsistent overrides") — at which point hand-written `if`/`else` code becomes the wrong engineering choice and a CSP solver becomes the right one, exactly the way `fundamentals-of-software-architecture-richards-ford.md`'s "fitness function" concept named a pattern (`tdd-gate`, lint-rule asides) this repo already practiced without naming.
- **Bayesian networks are a rigorous, complementary framing to Raschka's softmax-calibration finding — not a restatement of it.** `build-llm-from-scratch-raschka.md`'s central finding (already read in full, and not to be repeated here) is that a neural classifier's "confidence score" is an uncalibrated softmax output, fixed post-hoc by temperature/Platt scaling against a held-out labeled set. AIMA's probabilistic-reasoning chapters (Ch. 12-14) describe a structurally different path to a confidence number: a Bayesian network represents uncertainty as an explicit graph of named variables and stated conditional-probability tables (e.g., `P(document is Restricted | contains SSN, contains HEALTH_TERM, source=Google Drive)`), where the resulting posterior probability has principled meaning *by construction* — it follows from declared conditional-independence assumptions and specified priors, not from calibrating an opaque network's output after the fact. The practical difference for an auditor: a calibrated softmax score is a well-calibrated *number*, but still not decomposable into "which upstream factors, weighted how, produced this score" — a Bayesian network's posterior is decomposable into exactly that, edge by edge. This is a genuine second axis (explainability of the *mechanism*, not just calibration of the *output*) that neither Raschka's file nor any of the other three raised, because none of them consider a non-neural technique for producing an uncertainty estimate at all.
- **Naive Bayes is AIMA's own worked example of a cheap, small-data, per-feature-inspectable classifier — a third frugal alternative alongside prompting and Raschka's fine-tuned classification head.** AIMA's Naive Bayes chapter treats each feature's contribution to a classification decision as a named conditional probability that can be read off directly (`P(HEALTH_TERM present | Confidential)` vs. `P(HEALTH_TERM present | Public)`), trainable from a small labeled set with no GPU and no deep-learning toolchain at all. For a lower-stakes classification decision where full interpretability matters more than squeezing out the last few points of accuracy, this is a real, frugal, and CLAUDE.md-aligned ("prefer simpler solutions... when outcomes are equivalent") option this repo currently has no vocabulary for — distinct from Raschka's classification-head fine-tuning (a small neural network, still an opaque function once trained) and from a prompted LLM call (no training at all, but also no per-feature inspectability).
- **GDPR Article 22 — automated individual decision-making, including profiling — is a real, concrete, and currently absent control category, and this book's ethics/safety chapters supply the regulatory hook that makes explainability a compliance requirement, not just good engineering practice.** `skills/compliance-design/SKILL.md`'s GDPR scope (read in full, "Compliance Frameworks" table, line 34) names Articles 5, 25, and 32; its Control Decomposition section works through Article 32 in detail. **Article 22 — the data subject's right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing that produces legal or similarly significant effects, and the associated right to meaningful information about the logic involved — is named nowhere in the skill.** This product's core function is exactly this kind of automated decision: `data-classification`'s sensitivity assignment can determine what access, retention, or erasure treatment a data subject's information receives, with real consequences, computed automatically before any human reviews it (below-threshold cases are flagged for review, but passed, above-threshold cases are not). AIMA's discussion of AI transparency and accountability is the source that surfaces this specific gap; the fix — a control requiring that any solely-automated compliance decision be reconstructable and explainable on request — is exactly what deterministic rule-based logic or a Bayesian network's decomposable posterior can satisfy, and what a calibrated-but-opaque neural classifier cannot.
- **Utility theory formalizes, but does not add new content to, book 1's already-raised asymmetric-error-cost concept — flagged here explicitly to avoid double-counting.** AIMA's decision-theory chapters (expected utility, utility functions over outcomes) are the formal mathematics underneath `ai-engineering-huyen.md`'s "asymmetric error cost is a first-class evaluation design input" finding (already applied to `data-classification`'s and `data-quality-rules`' confidence thresholds). This is confirmation, not new ground — see Caveats.
- **The large majority of this book's content — classical heuristic search (A*, minimax and game-tree search), general first-order-logic theorem proving, STRIPS-style automated planning, the full robotics and computer-vision chapters, and most of its natural-language-processing material — has no plausible, honest yield for this product.** This repo has no game-playing, no path-planning, no general theorem-proving obligation, and no robotics component; its NLP need (document classification, entity extraction) is already fully covered, more directly and more currently, by the other four books in this cluster. Per this file's honesty mandate (matching `build-llm-from-scratch-raschka.md`'s discipline of proposing zero candidates when a book's content does not clear the bar), these chapters are named and set aside rather than force-fit into a skill proposal — see Caveats for the full list.

## Actionable Techniques

- For any compliance decision where an auditor will plausibly ask "why was this flagged" — not just document-level classification, but any Restricted-tier or access-affecting automated decision — evaluate deterministic rule-based logic (forward-chaining production rules) or a Bayesian network as an architecture alternative *before* defaulting to an ML classifier or LLM prompt, and record which was chosen and why. This is a new, explicit criterion (explainability-as-driving-requirement) currently missing from `ai-engineering-huyen.md`'s proposed `llm-classification-design` technique-selection table (cost/latency/data-requirement/accuracy-ceiling only).
- Add **GDPR Article 22** as a named control category in `compliance-design`'s Control Decomposition, alongside the existing Articles 5, 25, 30, and 32 — decomposed into a specific behaviour ("no solely-automated decision with legal/significant effect is made without either a documented reconstruction path or a human-in-the-loop review point") and a verifiable check (an audit trail sufficient to reconstruct, on request, which rules, constraints, or Bayesian factors produced a given classification decision).
- When a Bayesian network is used anywhere in this product for uncertainty representation, require the network's structure (named variables and their conditional dependencies) and the specific conditional-probability values used for a given inference to be included in that decision's audit trail — not just the resulting posterior probability. This is a different requirement from Raschka's calibration fix: a calibrated softmax score can be a trustworthy *number* while still being undecomposable into named contributing factors; a Bayesian posterior is decomposable by construction, and the audit trail should capture that decomposition, not just the final figure.
- For the entity types `data-classification`'s own detection table already implies are deterministic (`EMAIL`, `PHONE`, format-checkable `SSN`/`IBAN`/`ACCOUNT_NUMBER` patterns), name the technique explicitly as rule-based pattern matching — formally, a degenerate CSP with no search required — closing the specific piece of book 1's "opaque verb" finding that applies to the cases already, correctly, not using a model at all.
- Before choosing an ML classifier (fine-tuned or prompted) over deterministic rule-based logic for any Restricted-tier compliance decision, require an ADR stating why deterministic rules or a Bayesian network could not sufficiently express the decision — mirroring the "prompting-first, fine-tuning only with an ADR" escalation discipline `ai-engineering-huyen.md` already established, applied one level earlier: deterministic-logic-first, an ML technique only with an ADR showing rules genuinely cannot express the judgment (e.g., a judgment requiring reading unstructured prose for tone or context that no finite rule set can enumerate).
- If `data-classification`'s propagation formula (the max rule, override-outside-max, escalation-triggers-review) ever grows more complex — additional special categories, cross-asset constraints, or conflicting steward overrides across related assets — formalize it explicitly as a CSP (named variables, named constraints, a stated solving strategy) rather than accreting `if`/`else` branches, and add a CSP-solver dependency decision to the same escalation discipline `data-pipeline-implementation`'s Python Exception already models for other non-default toolchain choices.
- Do not use "Bayesian network" and "calibrated confidence score" interchangeably in any future skill's glossary or worked example: name explicitly, for every reported confidence/probability figure in the product, whether it is (a) a raw or calibrated softmax output from a neural classifier (Raschka's territory), (b) a posterior probability from a Bayesian network with a stated, inspectable structure (this book's territory), or (c) neither — a rule simply fired or did not (no probability at all). Conflating these three in a single "confidence" column is the specific anti-pattern this technique guards against.

## Key Terminology

Worth considering for `skills/glossary-management/`'s Ubiquitous Language only if `explainable-decision-logic-design` is actually built. None of these duplicate the four prior files' terms (`Foundation Model`, `Prompt Engineering`, `RAG`, `Golden Dataset`, `Hallucination`, `Model/Prompt Version`, `Guardrails`; `Data Drift`, `Concept Drift`, `Model Staleness`, `Train/Serve Skew`, `Natural Label`, `Class Imbalance`, `Shadow Deployment`, `Champion/Challenger`, `Training-Data Provenance`; `LLMOps`, `FTI Pipeline Architecture`, `LoRA`, `QLoRA`, `Instruction Dataset`, `Model Registry`, `Prompt/Dataset/Model Versioning as CI/CD Artifacts`, `Quantization`, `Continuous (Dynamic) Batching`, `KV Cache`; `Byte Pair Encoding`, `Self-Attention`, `Logit`, `Softmax`, `Calibration (Temperature Scaling)`, `Context Window`, `Classification Head`):

- **Constraint Satisfaction Problem (CSP)** — a problem expressed as a set of variables, each with a domain of possible values, and a set of constraints restricting which combinations of values are allowed; solved by search techniques (backtracking, arc consistency) rather than statistical inference. The formal name for the deterministic computation `data-classification`'s propagation formula (the max rule, override-outside-max) already implements without naming it.
- **Forward Chaining / Backward Chaining** — the two directions of inference in a rule-based (production-rule) system: forward chaining starts from known facts and fires rules whose conditions are satisfied to derive new facts; backward chaining starts from a goal (a hypothesis to prove or a decision to justify) and works backward to the facts that would support it. The mechanism underneath `explainable-decision-logic-design`'s audit-trail-by-construction claim — a fired rule and the fact that triggered it are the audit trail, not a separately bolted-on log.
- **Expert System** — a rule-based system encoding domain expert knowledge as explicit if-then rules, historically AI's dominant paradigm before statistical machine learning; the direct architectural ancestor of a forward-chaining compliance-decision engine.
- **Bayesian Network** — a directed graph of random variables where each node's conditional probability table states its dependence on its parent variables, and the network's structure encodes stated conditional-independence assumptions; a decision's posterior probability is decomposable, edge by edge, into the specific factors and probabilities that produced it. The principled, inspectable-by-construction counterpart to a calibrated neural softmax score (Raschka's `Calibration (Temperature Scaling)` term) — the two are not the same kind of "probability" and should never be labeled with an undifferentiated "confidence" column.
- **Prior Probability / Posterior Probability** — a Bayesian network's belief in a variable before observing evidence (prior) versus after (posterior, via Bayes' rule). The vocabulary needed to state precisely what a Bayesian-network-derived confidence figure actually represents, as distinct from a softmax output.
- **Naive Bayes** — a simple Bayesian classifier assuming conditional independence between features given the class; cheap to train from small labeled sets, and each feature's contribution to a classification is a named, directly inspectable conditional probability. A third frugal, small-data classification option alongside prompting (book 1) and a fine-tuned classification head (Raschka), distinguished by per-feature interpretability rather than raw accuracy.
- **Automated Individual Decision-Making (GDPR Article 22)** — the regulatory term for a decision based solely on automated processing (including profiling) that produces legal or similarly significant effects on a data subject, triggering a right to meaningful information about the logic involved. The specific compliance hook that makes "can this decision be explained" a named control requirement for this product, not merely good engineering practice.

## Caveats

- **The large majority of this book's content is genuinely out of scope for this repo, and this file says so plainly rather than force-fitting it.** Classical heuristic and adversarial search (A*, minimax, alpha-beta pruning, Monte Carlo tree search — this repo has no game-playing or path-planning problem), general first-order-logic theorem proving beyond the narrow rule-based-inference use named above, STRIPS-style automated planning, the robotics chapters (kinematics, motion planning, SLAM), and the bulk of the NLP chapters (parsing, machine translation, speech) have no honest yield here — this product's actual language-processing need (document classification, entity extraction) is already covered more directly and more currently by the other four books in this cluster. Reinforcement learning and Markov Decision Processes are likewise not actionable: this repo has no sequential-decision/control problem anywhere in its roster, and nothing in the product's stated scope (data estate mapping and compliance) resembles an RL setting.
- **This book is not primarily about LLMs, and later editions only lightly touch the modern LLM era — do not read it as a fifth LLM-engineering book.** Its treatment of neural networks and deep learning is a general-purpose survey chapter, not the mechanistic depth `build-llm-from-scratch-raschka.md` already supplied for transformers specifically, nor the applied engineering depth the other three books supplied for using foundation models. Where this file's content overlaps with the neural-network material at all (Naive Bayes vs. a classification head, Bayesian posteriors vs. softmax scores), it is offered as a genuinely different technique family, not a restatement or an update of the other four books' neural-network content.
- **The explainability angle this file proposes is conditional on a real, specific compliance need — not a mandate to replace ML classifiers generally.** Per `ai-engineering-huyen.md`'s own caveat (still correct here): deterministic detection for genuinely pattern-based signals (EMAIL, SSN format) is already, plausibly, the right technique, and the semantic judgments (is this document's tone Confidential?) are exactly where a model earns its cost. This book's contribution is a *third* option for the narrow but real slice of decisions where an auditor or a GDPR Article 22 data subject specifically needs the reasoning shown, not a general argument that rule-based systems out-perform or should replace ML classifiers as a default.
- **A Bayesian network is still a real design and maintenance commitment, not a free lunch relative to a calibrated classifier.** Building one requires stating a network structure and conditional-probability tables — either hand-authored from domain expertise (itself a real design effort with its own risk of being wrong) or learned from data (which reintroduces a training-data-provenance obligation not unlike a classical classifier's). Treat this file's Bayesian-network proposal as a genuine alternative worth an ADR-gated evaluation for explainability-critical decisions specifically, not as a strictly cheaper or strictly better default than prompting or fine-tuning.
- **The GDPR Article 22 gap is a real, concrete finding grounded in what was actually read in `compliance-design/SKILL.md`, and should be raised as a direct proposal, not silently patched into the skill.** Per this repo's own working discipline (chunk-by-chunk approval before building), adding a new control category to an existing, already-shipped skill is a content change that needs Shafi's explicit review — this file surfaces the gap and its regulatory grounding; it does not resolve it.
- **As with all four prior books in this cluster: do not confuse this with the `research/ai-agent-engineering/` cluster.** That cluster is about how this factory's own Claude Code agents/skills are engineered; this book, like the four before it in `ai-ml-engineering/`, is about the *product's* own use of AI/ML/logic-based reasoning to classify and reason about customer documents. Different layer, never cross-cited as answering the same question.
