# Data Modeling Made Simple
**Author:** Steve Hoberman
**Domain:** Data & Analytics — Foundational Data Modeling

## Core Concepts

- **The three-level model progression: Conceptual → Logical → Physical.** The book's organizing idea is that a data model is built in three deliberately separate passes, each answering a different question and aimed at a different audience: the **Conceptual Data Model (CDM)** captures *what the business talks about* — the high-level entities and their relationships, in business language, with no keys or data types, reviewable by a non-technical stakeholder in one sitting; the **Logical Data Model (LDM)** adds full rigor — every attribute, every relationship's cardinality and optionality, primary/foreign/alternate keys, normalization — still technology-independent; the **Physical Data Model (PDM)** is the LDM translated into a specific database's actual tables, columns, data types, and performance-driven denormalization. Skipping straight to physical design (deciding column types and indexes before the business concepts and their relationships are agreed) is the book's central failure mode — the same shape of mistake Kimball warns against for skipping the grain, but one layer earlier and one layer more general: Hoberman's concern is *any* relational model, not specifically the BI presentation layer.

- **Entity, Attribute, Relationship as the base ER vocabulary.** An **Entity** is a person, place, thing, concept, or event the business needs to hold information about (`Customer`, `Order`, `Product`) — deliberately a *business* noun, not yet a table. An **Attribute** is a property of an entity (`Customer.email`). A **Relationship** is a business rule connecting two entities, always expressed with a verb phrase in both directions (`Customer places Order` / `Order is placed by Customer`).

- **Cardinality and optionality, read as English sentences.** Every relationship carries two properties that must be stated explicitly, not left implicit in a diagram: **cardinality** (how many — one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many) and **optionality/modality** (whether the relationship is mandatory or optional on each side). Hoberman's signature technique is to make every relationship readable as a plain sentence a business stakeholder can verify without any notation literacy: *"Each Customer may place one or more Orders. Each Order must be placed by exactly one Customer."* The words "may"/"must" carry optionality; "one"/"one or more" carries cardinality. A relationship that cannot be read this way in front of the business owner has not actually been validated — only drawn.

- **Keys: primary, alternate, and foreign.** The **primary key** uniquely identifies an entity instance; an **alternate key** (candidate key) is a different attribute or combination that could also serve as identifier but wasn't chosen (e.g. `email` when `customer_id` is primary); a **foreign key** is a primary key value copied into another entity to implement a relationship. The book treats key selection as a business decision made at the logical level — not an accident of whatever the physical database defaults to.

- **Normalization as a logical-model discipline, not a physical mandate.** The book covers normalization (through 3NF, generally) as the mechanism for eliminating redundancy and the update/insert/delete anomalies redundancy causes — each fact stored in exactly one place, keyed by the whole key and nothing but the key. Critically, Hoberman frames denormalization as a *conscious, physical-layer-only* trade for performance, made after the logical model is fully normalized — never a substitute for doing the normalization pass in the first place.

- **Subtypes and supertypes (generalization/specialization).** When several entities share common attributes and relationships but also have their own distinct ones, the book models a **supertype** (`Party`) holding the shared attributes and **subtypes** (`Person`, `Organization`) holding what's distinct — with a documented discriminator (a "subtype rule": exclusive/inclusive, complete/incomplete) governing which subtypes can co-occur. This is the classic technique for entities that are "the same kind of thing, described differently by different parts of the business."

- **Naming standards as a first-class deliverable.** The book devotes real attention to entity and attribute naming conventions — consistent class words/suffixes (`_ID`, `_CODE`, `_NAME`, `_DESCRIPTION`, `_DATE`), singular entity names, no embedded abbreviations invented ad hoc — on the grounds that an inconsistently named model is unreviewable and silently drifts from the Ubiquitous Language it's supposed to encode.

- **The definition as a testable artifact.** Every entity and attribute must carry a complete, business-reviewable definition — not a restatement of the name. Hoberman is emphatic that "Customer: a customer" is not a definition; a real definition explains what makes something qualify as an instance of the entity, states what's excluded, and can be checked by a business person who has never seen the diagram.

- **Model validation as a review ritual, not a sign-off formality.** The book's recurring workflow is: build the model, then *walk it* with the actual business stakeholders — reading every relationship sentence aloud, confirming every entity definition, checking every attribute against a real example — before the model is treated as agreed. This is presented as the step most commonly skipped under schedule pressure, and the one whose absence causes the most expensive rework.

## Actionable Techniques

- Before any physical schema, produce (even briefly) a Conceptual Data Model: entity names and relationship verb phrases only, no keys, no types — something a non-technical reviewer can approve in one sitting.
- For every relationship, write its cardinality/optionality as two plain sentences ("Each X must/may [verb] one/one-or-more Y") and have the actual business owner confirm both directions before the relationship is finalized.
- Give every entity and every attribute a real definition: what qualifies, what's explicitly excluded, and one concrete example — reject "the name restated" as a definition.
- Run a normalization pass at the logical level before any denormalization decision; document each denormalization as a deliberate, physical-only, performance-justified exception, not a default.
- Apply a consistent naming standard (class words/suffixes, singular entity nouns) across every entity and attribute, and reject ad hoc abbreviations that don't appear in the glossary.
- When two or more entities share attributes/relationships but also diverge, model a supertype/subtype pair with an explicit subtype discriminator rule (exclusive vs. inclusive, complete vs. incomplete) rather than duplicating shared attributes across siblings.
- Hold an explicit model-walkthrough review with the business stakeholder as a scheduled step — not an implicit byproduct of a design document being shared — and treat unresolved cardinality/optionality disagreements found there as blocking, not advisory.
- Score a finished model against a short quality checklist before calling it done: does every entity/attribute have a business-reviewable definition; does every relationship read as a valid sentence in both directions; is the model free of entities/attributes with no defined purpose; is it consistent with names/definitions used elsewhere in the same domain. (Hoberman later formalized a fuller "Data Model Scorecard" with ten weighted criteria — treat the specific ten-item list as a related-book detail, not asserted here with full confidence; the checklist habit itself is squarely this book's content.)

## Key Terminology

Candidates for `glossary-management`'s Ubiquitous Language if a conceptual/logical modeling vocabulary is adopted: **Conceptual Data Model (CDM)**, **Logical Data Model (LDM)**, **Physical Data Model (PDM)**, **Cardinality**, **Optionality (Modality)**, **Alternate Key**, **Subtype / Supertype**, **Normalization / Normal Form**, **Data Model Scorecard** (lower confidence as a term this specific book formalizes vs. a related later Hoberman work — see Caveats). **Entity**, **Attribute**, and **Relationship** are already effectively in use informally across the data-architect's skills but are not glossary-defined in this ER-modeling sense — see the Caveats entry below before adding them, since "Entity" already has a distinct, incompatible DDD meaning in this repo's glossary.

## Caveats

- **The DDD/ER "Entity" collision is real and must be handled explicitly, not glossed over.** In this repo's Ubiquitous Language, `Entity` means a specific DDD concept: an object with identity and a lifecycle, distinguished from a Value Object, living inside an Aggregate (see `CLAUDE.md`'s Ubiquitous Language list and `data-model-design`'s Aggregate-to-Schema Rule). In Hoberman's ER vocabulary, `Entity` means something much broader and shallower: any noun the business tracks data about — closer to what this repo calls a "concept" or "table candidate," and covering things (like `DataSource`, `Person`, `Organisation` as canonical master-data concepts) that may or may not correspond to a DDD Entity, and could just as easily be a Value Object, an Aggregate Root, or a purely canonical/integration-layer concept with no DDD status at all. If this book's terminology is adopted for a conceptual/logical modeling step, it must use a different term — e.g. "ER-Entity" or "conceptual entity" — never bare "Entity," or every future reference to "Entity" in this repo becomes ambiguous between two incompatible meanings. `extracted_entities` (the table in `data-model-design`) is itself a preview of the collision: it's a DDD child Entity that also happens to model `PERSON`/`EMAIL`/etc. "entities" in the ER/NLP sense — two different senses of the word already coexisting in one artifact.
- **ER relationships are not Aggregate boundaries and must not be treated as license to add foreign keys across them.** A straight ER modeling pass looks at two related concepts and draws a relationship, which physically becomes a foreign key. `data-model-design`'s Aggregate-to-Schema Rule explicitly forbids foreign keys across Aggregate boundaries (cross-Aggregate references are plain UUID columns; the domain enforces integrity, not the database). Any conceptual/logical modeling step adopted from this book must be filtered through that rule: a CDM/LDM relationship between two Aggregates documents a *business* relationship and its cardinality/optionality, but it does not automatically justify a database-level FK — the Aggregate boundary always wins over what a pure ER model would draw.
- **Normalization discipline here is about the operational/OLTP layer, not the BI/dimensional layer already covered by the Kimball research file.** Where Kimball's dimensional modeling deliberately denormalizes dimension tables for the presentation/BI layer, Hoberman's normalization guidance applies to the transactional model — largely already implicit in `data-model-design`'s Aggregate-to-table mapping (child tables, FK-cascade, no repeated groups) but never named as "normalization" or checked against normal-form reasoning explicitly. The two books' denormalization stances are not in conflict — they denormalize different layers for different reasons — but naming both correctly (operational normalization vs. dimensional denormalization) avoids a reader conflating them.
- **This book predates and is independent of DDD** (Hoberman's book, 1st edition 2005 and 2nd edition 2009, vs. Evans' *Domain-Driven Design*, 2003 — contemporaneous, not derived from each other). It offers no guidance on Bounded Contexts, Aggregates, or Domain Events, and its "Entity" vocabulary was never designed with DDD's stricter Entity/Value-Object/Aggregate distinctions in mind — hence the collision above.
- **Confidence level:** solid on the CDM/LDM/PDM progression, ER base vocabulary (Entity/Attribute/Relationship/Key), the cardinality-and-optionality-as-sentences validation technique, the normalization-as-logical/denormalization-as-physical framing, subtyping, naming standards, and the definition-completeness discipline — these are the book's consistently well-documented, load-bearing content. Lower confidence on exact chapter structure and on whether the fully-formalized "Data Model Scorecard" (the ten-weighted-criteria version widely cited today) originates in this book's edition or is more fully developed in Hoberman's later, dedicated *Data Model Scorecard* book — flagged inline above rather than asserted as this book's content.
