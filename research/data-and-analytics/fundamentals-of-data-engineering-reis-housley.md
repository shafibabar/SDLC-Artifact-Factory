# Fundamentals of Data Engineering
**Authors:** Joe Reis and Matt Housley
**Domain:** Data & Analytics — Data Engineering Lifecycle

## Core Concepts

- **The Data Engineering Lifecycle.** The book's central framework, replacing the old "ETL pipeline" mental model with five stages a data engineer is accountable for end to end: **Generation** (data is produced at a source system), **Storage** (the substrate everything else reads from and writes to, present at every stage, not just "the warehouse"), **Ingestion** (getting data from source systems into the platform), **Transformation** (turning raw data into a usable, business-logic-bearing form), and **Serving** (analytics, machine learning, and reverse ETL — the point where data produces value). The stages are not strictly linear — storage underlies all of them, and transformation can happen at multiple points.

- **The Undercurrents.** Six cross-cutting disciplines run through every lifecycle stage and are, per the authors, where most of the real engineering judgment lives: **Security**, **Data Management** (governance, data quality, master data management, ethics/privacy — largely drawn from the DAMA-DMBOK framework), **DataOps** (DevOps applied to data pipelines: automation, observability/monitoring, incident response), **Data Architecture** (the discipline of making deliberate, reversible, scalable design trade-offs), **Orchestration** (scheduling and dependency management across the whole lifecycle, not just within one pipeline), and **Software Engineering** (version control, testing, infrastructure as code, applied to pipeline code the same as any other software). The book's explicit thesis: "data engineering is 90% architecture and undercurrents, 10% tools" — tool choice is a late, narrow decision, not the starting point.

- **The data engineer's role has shifted from ETL-builder to lifecycle owner.** The book frames the historical data engineer as a narrow pipeline-plumbing role (extract, transform, load, repeat) and argues the modern role now spans the *whole* lifecycle: understanding source systems well enough to influence how they emit data (upstream), and increasingly overlapping with analytics engineering and ML engineering on the serving side (downstream). The job is no longer "move data from A to B" — it's "own the data's fitness for use across its entire life."

- **Good Data Architecture principles.** The book lays out a working list of architecture principles rather than a fixed methodology: always add business value, choose common components wisely (standardize where it saves effort, without becoming inflexible), plan for failure (assume components will fail; design for graceful degradation), architect for scalability (elastic, not just "big enough today"), treat architecture as a leadership responsibility (not a diagram nobody consults), keep "always be architecting" as a continuous practice rather than a one-time phase, build loosely coupled systems, prefer reversible decisions over ones that lock in a technology or vendor, prioritize security by default, and embrace FinOps (treat cloud/infrastructure cost as a first-class, continuously managed variable, not an afterthought).

- **Batch vs. streaming is a trade-off to justify, not a default.** The book pushes back on "just make it streaming" as a reflex: true low-latency streaming (sub-second) carries real complexity and cost (exactly-once semantics, stateful stream processing, operational burden) that most use cases don't actually need. The practical middle ground the book repeatedly returns to is **micro-batch** — small, frequent batches — which gets most of the perceived benefit of "real-time" at a fraction of the engineering cost. The right question is always "what latency does the *business decision* this data supports actually require," not "what's the most impressive architecture."

- **Data quality, framed loosely around three characteristics.** Where the book does discuss data quality directly, it's framed around **accuracy** (does the data reflect reality), **completeness** (is all the data that should exist present), and **timeliness** (is the data available when needed) — a lighter, three-part framing than the fuller six-dimension DAMA-DMBOK model (which additionally names consistency, validity, and uniqueness). The book treats data quality less as a standalone checklist and more as an outcome of good architecture, good orchestration, and — critically — **data observability**: continuously monitoring freshness, volume, distribution, schema, and lineage in production, rather than only validating individual records at ingestion time.

- **Metadata has three categories.** The book classifies metadata as **technical metadata** (schemas, lineage, data types, partitioning — how the data is structured and where it flows), **business metadata** (definitions, business rules, glossary terms, data ownership — what the data *means*), and **operational metadata** (job run statistics, execution times, success/failure rates, records processed — how the pipeline that produced the data actually behaved). Good data management requires capturing all three, not just the technical kind engineers default to.

- **Reverse ETL and "data products."** On the serving side, the book names **reverse ETL** — pushing transformed, analytics-layer data back into operational systems (e.g., a CRM) so business teams act on it in the tools they already use — as an increasingly common fourth serving pattern alongside BI, ML, and ad hoc analytics. It frames the end goal of the whole lifecycle as producing **data products**: data assets built and maintained with the same ownership, discoverability, and reliability expectations as any other product a team ships, not a byproduct of "whatever the warehouse happens to contain."

- **A Data Maturity Model.** The book sketches a rough progression — from ad hoc, reactive data work, through a "scaling" stage where processes formalize, to a "leading" stage where data is a core, well-governed product capability — mainly to help a team calibrate how much process/tooling investment is proportionate to where they actually are, not to prescribe a fixed target state for every organization.

## Actionable Techniques

- Before designing or reviewing any data component, name which of the five lifecycle stages it belongs to (generation / storage / ingestion / transformation / serving) — a component that silently mixes ingestion and transformation concerns is a smell the book explicitly warns about.
- Run every architecture decision through the undercurrents checklist: does this design account for security, data management (quality/governance/lineage), DataOps (automation/observability/incident response), orchestration, and software engineering practice (tests, version control) — not just "does it move data correctly."
- Treat tool/technology selection as the *last* design decision, made only after the lifecycle stage and undercurrent requirements are clear — never the first.
- For any proposed streaming component, write down the actual business-decision latency requirement first, and check whether micro-batch would meet it before committing to true low-latency stream processing.
- For any data quality effort, distinguish "checking records at ingestion" from "observability of the pipeline in production" (freshness, volume, distribution, schema, lineage) — the book treats the second as the more durable investment.
- When designing metadata capture for any dataset, explicitly ask whether technical, business, *and* operational metadata are all covered — not just the technical/schema kind that's easiest to automate.
- When a metric, dashboard, or dataset is meant for another team or system to consume, treat it as a data product: name its owner, its freshness/reliability SLA, and how a consumer discovers it — don't let it exist as an implicit byproduct of a pipeline built for something else.
- Periodically reassess architecture decisions ("always be architecting") rather than treating a design doc as permanent once approved — and prefer decisions that are cheap to reverse over ones that lock in a vendor or pattern.
- Track infrastructure/pipeline cost as a living metric (FinOps) alongside correctness and latency, especially for anything that scales with data volume or estate size.

## Key Terminology

Candidates worth considering for `glossary-management`'s Ubiquitous Language:

- **Data Engineering Lifecycle** and its five stages (**Generation, Storage, Ingestion, Transformation, Serving**) — a useful organizing vocabulary currently absent; this repo's pipeline maps onto it but never names it.
- **Undercurrent** — the book's term for a cross-cutting concern (Security, Data Management, DataOps, Data Architecture, Orchestration, Software Engineering); could clarify why, e.g., `metrics-instrumentation-plan` and `opentelemetry-instrumentation` are both "instrumentation" but serve different lifecycle purposes.
- **Data Observability** (and its five pillars: freshness, distribution, volume, schema, lineage) — genuinely missing; the closest existing concept is `data-quality-rules`' per-record gating, which is a different (complementary, not overlapping) practice.
- **Technical Metadata / Business Metadata / Operational Metadata** — a clean three-way split this repo's skills currently implement in practice (across `data-lineage-design`, `data-classification`, `metrics-instrumentation-plan`) but never name as a shared taxonomy.
- **Data Product** — relevant to how `metrics-instrumentation-plan` and `dashboard-specification` frame consumer-facing deliverables; not currently a term this repo uses.
- **FinOps** — relevant to `data-retention-policy`'s storage lifecycle and to `data-pipeline-design`'s scaling/partitioning decisions; not currently named anywhere in the tech stack or governance skills.

**Not a new candidate:** *Change Data Capture* is already a canonical Ubiquitous Language term in `CLAUDE.md`'s event-driven terms list — the book uses it consistently with this repo's existing meaning, so no action needed there.

## Caveats

- This book is written for **general-purpose, often open-ended data platforms** — arbitrary source systems, a choice among data warehouse/data lake/lakehouse storage, an open set of BI and ML serving destinations. This repo's first product is the opposite of open-ended: a **fixed, event-driven, compliance-specific pipeline** with two named source connectors (Google Drive, S3), three named file formats (PDF/DOCX/XLSX), and a fixed sequence of stages ending in a compliance rule engine and audit log — not a general analytics platform. Most of the book's *storage-stage* content (warehouse vs. lake vs. lakehouse selection, columnar formats, query engines) simply doesn't apply here; PostgreSQL + Apache AGE is already the settled answer.
- The book's **generation-stage** guidance (engaging with source-system owners, influencing upstream schema design, CDC from owned databases) assumes the data engineer has some relationship with or control over the source system. Here, the sources are third-party customer storage (Google Drive, S3) the product has zero influence over — the generation stage is closer to "ingest an arbitrary, uncontrolled external system" than the book's typical case of a company's own upstream OLTP database.
- The book's **orchestration** undercurrent centers on DAG-based batch orchestration (Airflow-style tools, explicit dependency graphs, scheduling). This repo's pipeline is **event choreography** (`data-pipeline-design`), a deliberately different topology — stages react to events, there is no central DAG or scheduler coordinating them. The book's orchestration principles (visibility into dependencies, retry/backoff, failure isolation) still apply in spirit, but its concrete orchestration tooling recommendations do not transfer.
- The book's treatment of the **serving** stage leans heavily on BI tools, ML platforms, and reverse ETL into SaaS systems — largely out of scope for this product today (`ai-ml-architect` is explicitly deferred; there's no reverse-ETL destination in the MVP). `dashboard-specification` and `reporting-spec` already cover the realistic serving surface for this product; nothing here should push toward building general BI infrastructure that isn't needed.
- The book treats data engineering as **one role owning the whole lifecycle**. This repo deliberately splits that lifecycle across two agents — `data-architect` (design: pipeline topology, lineage design, classification scheme, retention policy) and `data-engineer` (implementation: stage workers, quality gates, metrics, dashboards/reports) — a division the book doesn't make but which is consistent with its "architecture is leadership, separate from execution" principle; nothing in the book argues against this split, but a reader should not expect the book's chapter boundaries to map one-to-one onto this repo's agent boundaries.
