# User Stories Applied: For Agile Software Development
**Author:** Mike Cohn
**Domain:** Requirements & User Stories

## Core Concepts

- **The Three Cs — Card, Conversation, Confirmation.** A story card is deliberately incomplete: it is a token for a conversation, not a specification. "Card" holds a short description; "Conversation" is where the real detail is negotiated between the team and the customer/persona, over time, not upfront; "Confirmation" is the set of conditions of satisfaction (what later tooling formalized as acceptance criteria) that prove the conversation's outcome was actually built. The point of the triad is to explicitly defer detail — writing a fully detailed card is treated as a failure mode, not thoroughness.
- **INVEST (coined by Bill Wake, adopted and popularized as a core chapter of this book).** Independent, Negotiable, Estimable, Small, Testable (Cohn's ordering is INVEST — Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable). Negotiable is the criterion most often lost in practice: a story is a placeholder for a requirement, not the requirement itself — writing implementation detail into the card defeats its purpose.
- **Story vs. traditional requirement.** Cohn's central thesis is that user stories exist as a reaction against big, detailed, upfront requirements documents (use cases, "shall" statements). A story is a promise to talk, sized for iterative planning, not a substitute for detailed documentation — but this book explicitly does not claim stories replace all documentation (safety-critical, regulated, or contractual detail may still need a traditional spec alongside the story).
- **User roles, not just personas.** Cohn's technique predates persona-driven design (Cooper). His method is "role brainstorming": list every way a distinct type of user interacts with the system, then consolidate near-duplicates and organize the surviving roles along axes that matter (novice vs. expert, frequent vs. occasional, in-house vs. external). Roles are a lighter-weight, more mechanical technique than full personas — useful specifically for generating the "As a ___" list before persona depth is available.
- **Story splitting patterns** (this is the most operational chapter of the book): split by workflow step, by business rule variation, by data boundary, by CRUD operation (create/read/update/delete, treated as a specific and separate pattern from generic "read vs. write"), by deferring performance/robustness to a later story, by breaking out a spike when the story is not estimable, and by separating the simple case from the complex case within the same capability.
- **Epics are just big, unsplit stories.** In this book, "epic" carries none of the outcome-hypothesis or OKR-linkage machinery seen in later Lean Startup-influenced practice — it is simply a story large enough that it must be split before it can be planned into an iteration. Cohn also uses "theme" as a lighter grouping label for a set of related stories/epics, mostly for release-level reporting.
- **Prioritization by value, cost, risk, and knowledge — not by category buckets.** Cohn's prioritization chapter weighs each story on four factors: financial/business value, cost to develop, amount of new knowledge or learning it produces, and amount of risk it removes. He also introduces the Kano model — Basic (threshold, expected) vs. Performance (linear, more-is-better) vs. Excitement (delighter) features — as a way to reason about *why* a feature matters, which is a different axis than "how much capacity is left." MoSCoW itself is **not** a Cohn/this-book technique — it originates from DSDM — so a skill citing this book for prioritization content would be citing the wrong source for that specific framework.
- **"Don't write novels" and premature detail as an anti-pattern.** A card with too much written detail is treated in the book as a warning sign — it means the conversation didn't happen, or happened too early and got frozen into text before the team had enough shared understanding to make the detail correct.

## Actionable Techniques

- **Three Cs checklist per story**: does the Card exist (short, on one line)? Has the Conversation actually happened (not just "assumed"? Is there a name attached to who was in it)? Are Confirmation conditions written and did the persona/customer agree to them?
- **Role brainstorming pass**: before writing stories, list every distinct way someone interacts with the system, merge duplicates, and organize by an axis that changes behavior (frequency, expertise, internal/external) — a fast, mechanical technique to run before or alongside deeper persona work.
- **CRUD-explicit splitting**: when a story bundles create/read/update/delete for one entity, split each operation into its own story rather than only splitting "read vs. write" as a pair.
- **Business-rule-variation split**: when a story's behavior differs by business rule (e.g., a discount rule, a compliance threshold), split each rule variation into its own story instead of writing conditional logic inside one story.
- **Defer-the-hard-part split**: split the simple/common case from the complex/rare case within one capability, shipping the simple version first even when the complex version is what a stakeholder asked for by default.
- **Four-factor prioritization score**: for any story not already forced by a Must-test, score it on value, cost, risk-removed, and knowledge-gained, and use the relative comparison — not a single subjective "priority" label — to break ties.
- **Kano tagging**: tag each story Basic / Performance / Excitement before prioritizing — Basic features that are missing generate disproportionate negative reaction relative to their "value," which a flat value/cost score alone will underweight.
- **Card brevity check**: if a story's written card requires more than a few lines to describe, that is itself a signal the story needs a conversation, not more writing.

- **Three Cs (Card, Conversation, Confirmation)** — foundational vocabulary for why stories are written the way they are; currently implicit, not named, anywhere in the read skills.
- **Conditions of Satisfaction** — the book's term for what this repo calls acceptance criteria; worth a glossary cross-reference so readers of the book's vocabulary and this repo's vocabulary map to each other.

## Caveats

- **MoSCoW is not from this book.** Do not cite Cohn as the source for the Must/Should/Could/Won't categories themselves — that's DSDM. Cohn's prioritization content (value/cost/risk/knowledge, Kano) is complementary but distinct, and should be added as a supplement to `moscow-prioritization`, not framed as "what the book already covers."
- **The book predates BDD/Gherkin.** Its "conditions of satisfaction" are informal bullet points; this repo's Given/When/Then formalization in `acceptance-criteria` is a legitimate, appropriate later addition (Gherkin/BDD tooling came from Chris Matts/Dan North, well after this book's 2004 publication) — no need to walk that back, just don't attribute the Gherkin format itself to Cohn.
- **The book predates persona-driven UX practice** in its mainstream form — its "user role" technique is a mechanical, lighter-weight precursor to personas, useful as a fallback but not a replacement for the deeper `user-persona` skill's output where that already exists.
- **"Epic" in this book means something narrower** than this repo's hypothesis-and-OKR-linked construct in `epic-definition`. Treat the repo's version as an intentional, documented adaptation for product-discovery rigor, not as "what Cohn calls an epic."
