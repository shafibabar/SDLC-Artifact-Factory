# Lean-Agile Acceptance Test-Driven-Development
**Author:** Ken Pugh
**Domain:** Requirements & User Stories (bridging to Testing)

## Core Concepts

- **ATDD is a collaboration practice first, a test-format second.** Pugh's central claim is that the value of ATDD comes from *who* writes the acceptance test and *when* — Customer (business), Developer, and Tester working out concrete examples together, before coding starts — not from the specific notation the test ends up in. This is the "triad" (Pugh's term is close to what later authors popularized as the "Three Amigos"): each role catches a different class of gap — the customer catches wrong business intent, the developer catches technical infeasibility or hidden complexity, the tester catches missing edge cases and ambiguity. A test written by only one role, however well-formatted, has skipped the part of ATDD that actually prevents rework.
- **ATDD ≠ BDD ≠ TDD — they overlap but answer different questions.** TDD is developer-facing and unit-level: it drives *implementation* design through Red-Green-Refactor, and the "customer" never sees a TDD test. BDD (Dan North) is a vocabulary and tooling discipline — Given/When/Then, "should" phrasing, tools like Cucumber that parse natural language into automation. ATDD (Pugh's frame, predating widespread Gherkin adoption and rooted in his own Fit/FitNesse work) is about the *process*: deriving the acceptance test collaboratively, before development, as the team's shared definition of "done" for a story — the notation could be Gherkin, could be a Fit/FitNesse table, could be plain declarative prose. Practically: BDD is a good notation for doing ATDD, but a team can do BDD-flavored Gherkin without ever holding the collaborative session ATDD requires, and get the format without the value.
- **Acceptance tests are the Confirmation in Ron Jeffries' Card/Conversation/Confirmation.** A user story card is a placeholder for a conversation; the acceptance test is the concrete, checkable record of what that conversation actually agreed to. Without it, "done" is renegotiated at delivery time based on whoever's memory of the conversation wins.
- **Specification by Example: examples come before rules, not after.** Pugh's discovery technique (independently converged on by Matt Wynne's Example Mapping and Gojko Adzic's later work) is to work from concrete example data first — real numbers, real names, real edge values — and let the general rule emerge from discussing several examples together. Working the other direction (write the abstract rule, then illustrate it with an example) tends to produce rules nobody has actually stress-tested against a hard case yet.
- **Declarative over imperative test style.** An acceptance test should say *what* is true of the business ("a Gold customer gets free shipping over $50"), not *how* to drive it through a UI ("click the cart icon, then click checkout, then..."). Imperative tests are brittle (every UI change breaks the spec) and obscure the actual business rule being tested. This is one of Pugh's most concrete, most quoted points.
- **A story isn't ready to estimate until it has at least one acceptance test.** If the triad can't produce a concrete example of the story working, the story isn't understood well enough to size — writing the acceptance test is diagnostic for readiness, not just downstream documentation.
- **Lean applied to requirements: eliminate translation waste.** Every re-statement of intent (business intent → written requirement → developer's interpretation → QA's interpretation → actual code) is a place understanding can leak. ATDD's goal is to collapse those hops: the example the business gave *is* the test that automates against the code, with no intermediate paraphrase.
- **The acceptance test is a contract, signed off before coding, changed only by agreement.** Once the triad agrees a test represents "done," a developer discovering it's wrong doesn't quietly reinterpret it — the change goes back through the same collaborative loop that created it.

## Actionable Techniques

- **Hold the test-writing conversation with all three roles present** (or, solo, deliberately think through all three perspectives) *before* estimation and *before* implementation — not as a post-hoc formality.
- **Start from concrete data, not the abstract rule.** Ask for a specific, real-feeling example first ("a customer named Maya with 3 files, one flagged Restricted") and only generalize to the rule after two or three examples surface the pattern and its exceptions.
- **Write the test declaratively.** Ban UI mechanics ("click," "type into the field") and technical mechanics ("PATCH the endpoint") from the test itself; push those into the automation layer underneath it.
- **Use the acceptance test as the estimation gate.** If the team cannot produce at least one worked example for a story, treat that as a signal the story isn't ready — not something to paper over with a rough estimate anyway.
- **Treat the acceptance test as the single, durable statement of "done."** Any later disagreement about whether the story is finished is resolved by re-reading the test, not by re-litigating the conversation from memory.
- **Changing an agreed acceptance test requires the same collaborative sign-off that created it** — a developer or tester silently "fixing" a test to match what got built defeats the entire practice.
- **Prefer a small number of examples that each isolate one rule** over one large combinatorial example that tests several rules at once — isolate the variable being tested (echoes equivalence partitioning / boundary analysis).

## Key Terminology

- **Specification by Example** is already present in CLAUDE.md's Testing ubiquitous-language table — good; this book is one of the source practices behind that term, alongside Adzic. No addition needed, just noting the provenance.
- **Acceptance Test-Driven Development (ATDD)** is not currently in the glossary or CLAUDE.md's methodology table, despite BDD and TDD both being listed there as non-negotiable methodologies. Given this repo's `acceptance-criteria` + `bdd-feature-file` pairing *is* an ATDD practice in substance (collaboratively-derived, pre-code acceptance tests later automated as Gherkin), the term is genuinely missing a name in this repo's vocabulary, not just an omission.
- **Three Amigos** — used informally in `example-mapping/SKILL.md`'s content and tags but never defined as a glossary term; worth adding since the repo already leans on the concept by name.
- **Declarative vs. Imperative (test style)** — the distinction is enforced as a rule in `bdd-feature-file` (banning imperative steps) but the pair of terms themselves aren't named anywhere as a glossary concept a PM could look up to understand *why* the rule exists.

## Caveats

- **Pugh is not the originator of BDD or the "Three Amigos" term** — BDD is Dan North's coinage; "Three Amigos" as a named workshop format is most associated with George Dinwiddie and popularized further by Adzic and Wynne. Pugh's contribution is the ATDD frame and its emphasis on the collaborative triad and declarative test style, developed substantially through his work on Fit/FitNesse. Treat this file's terminology carefully — don't attribute BDD's Given/When/Then vocabulary to this book.
- **Pugh's own preferred notation is often tabular (Fit/FitNesse tables), not Gherkin.** Gherkin/Cucumber already existed when this book was written (2011), so this isn't a "the book predates the format" situation the way the Hathaway requirements book does — but Pugh's worked examples lean tabular because of his personal tooling background. This repo's exclusive commitment to Gherkin is a reasonable, coherent modern choice; just don't assume Pugh mandates Gherkin specifically — his actual requirement is the *declarative, example-derived* property, which Gherkin satisfies but doesn't uniquely provide.
- **The "triad" (Customer/Developer/Tester) assumes live humans in a room.** This repo runs Ideate with a single `requirements-analyst` agent producing artifacts for Shafi's later approval — `example-mapping/SKILL.md` already handles this honestly by having the agent explicitly play all three perspectives in sequence rather than pretending a real multi-role conversation happened. The same honesty should extend to `acceptance-criteria`: if that skill adopts Pugh's "derive from a worked example" technique, it should say plainly that the "worked example" is the agent reasoning through Customer/Dev/Test perspectives alone, not evidence that a real collaborative session occurred.
- **Precise chapter/page-level specifics of this book are not claimed here with high confidence** — the concepts above reflect the well-established, widely-cited themes of Pugh's ATDD work (the triad, declarative-vs-imperative style, examples-before-rules, the acceptance test as a pre-code contract), not a verified line-by-line citation. Treat this file as reliable conceptual grounding for the refactor, not a page-referenced excerpt.
