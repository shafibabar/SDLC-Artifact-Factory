# Implementing Domain-Driven Design (Effective Aggregate Design)
**Authors:** Vaughn Vernon
**Domain:** Domain-Driven Design — Tactical Design, Aggregate Boundary Rules (the modern, prescriptive authority)

## Core Concepts

- **Vernon's four rules exist because Evans' single "invariant clustering" idea is under-specified for a working engineer — Vernon converts one principle into four checkable, sequenced tests (Ch. 10, originally the three-part "Effective Aggregate Design" InfoQ article Vernon later folded into the book).** Where Evans (`research/domain-driven-design/domain-driven-design-evans.md`) states that an Aggregate boundary should contain "the objects that participate in an invariant" and stops there, Vernon treats that statement as necessary but not sufficient — in his own account, teams that stop at Evans' framing reliably over-cluster, because almost anything *can* be argued to "participate in" some rule if the modeler doesn't also ask whether the rule is genuinely atomic. Vernon's four rules — Model True Invariants in Consistency Boundaries, Design Small Aggregates, Reference Other Aggregates by Identity, Use Eventual Consistency Outside the Boundary — are best read as one continuous argument with four checkpoints, not four independent rules a modeler can satisfy in any order. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s five rules restate the checkpoints (its Rule 1 ≈ Vernon Rule 1, Rule 5 ≈ Vernon Rule 2, Rule 2 ≈ Vernon Rule 3, Rule 3 ≈ Vernon Rule 4) but presents them as a flat, unordered list rather than Vernon's sequenced argument, and drops the connective reasoning between them that is the actual point of the book chapter.
- **Rule 1's real content is a negative test, not a positive one: "is this actually a true invariant that must hold at every instant, or a business rule that merely appears urgent and can tolerate being fixed up a moment later?"** Vernon's diagnostic move is to force the modeler to ask, for every candidate rule, whether a brief window of staleness would actually cause harm to the business — not whether it would be *surprising* or *inconvenient*. His own worked anti-example (the SaaSOvation Scrum-style product-management domain that recurs throughout the book) is a `Product` aggregate initially designed to directly contain every one of its `BacklogItem`s and, through those, every `Task` — the reasoning at design time was "a Product conceptually owns its backlog, so consistency of the whole backlog against the product should be guaranteed." Vernon walks through why this is wrong: nothing in the business actually requires that adding a `Task` to a `BacklogItem` be atomically consistent with every other `BacklogItem` under the same `Product`, or with the `Product`'s own attributes — the appearance of an invariant was really just conceptual composition ("Product *has* BacklogItems") mistaken for a transactional requirement. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s Rule 1 example ("A DataAsset may only be marked Restricted if its storage source is confirmed active") is a *correctly* identified true invariant by Vernon's own test — the check has to be atomic with the classify operation or a race window lets an asset get classified against a now-inactive source — but the skill gives the modeler no worked *counter*-example of a rule that looks like an invariant but isn't, which is precisely the harder, more common mistake Vernon spends the most space on.
- **Vernon's own three-question test for "is this a true invariant": (1) would violating this rule, even momentarily, cause real business harm if left uncorrected? (2) does correcting a violation after the fact require anything more than a compensating action a human or process could reasonably perform? (3) is the rule actually enforced by an external system already (e.g., a payment gateway, a downstream approval step) such that the aggregate re-enforcing it atomically is redundant?** A "yes" to (1) and "no" to (2)/(3) is the only combination that earns a place inside a single consistency boundary. Applied to this repo's own domain, a rule like "a DataAsset's extracted-entity count must never exceed some display cap" would fail this test (display-layer concern, not a true invariant); "a DataAsset cannot be Restricted while its storage source shows Inactive" (the skill's own example) passes it, assuming inactive-source classification would genuinely mislead a compliance reviewer if left uncorrected even briefly.
- **Rule 2 (Design Small Aggregates) is argued from three independent angles Vernon keeps distinct on purpose: scalability, performance, and collaboration — not one blended "small is good" intuition.** *Performance*: loading an Aggregate means loading its entire object graph in one round trip; a large graph (Vernon's Product-contains-everything example) means every single-field mutation pays the cost of hydrating thousands of unrelated child objects. *Scalability*: a large Aggregate held in memory and locked for a transaction constrains how many operations the system can process concurrently, because contention is measured per Aggregate *instance*, and a large Aggregate concentrates far more of the system's total write traffic onto one instance's lock/version than a correctly-split design would. *Collaboration*: multiple users (or automated processes) working on what feels like "the same area of the domain" collide far more often when that area is one Aggregate instance than when it's several independently-versioned ones — Vernon frames this explicitly as a multi-user, real-world-team concern, not merely a database-performance concern. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s "Sizing Under Contention" section (added in a later version per its own `1.1.0` numbering) already captures the performance/scalability angles well, but doesn't separately name the *collaboration* angle — the idea that oversized Aggregates fail specifically because multiple legitimate, non-conflicting user actions get serialized against each other artificially, not because the database is slow.
- **Vernon's explicit anti-pattern name for the failure mode Rule 2 guards against: modeling "the whole Bounded Context" (or a large swath of it) as a single Aggregate, out of a mistaken belief that doing so is "safer" because everything related is guaranteed consistent together.** He is emphatic that this instinct is almost always wrong, and that it is the single most common mistake he has personally seen in DDD projects and consulting engagements — worse than under-sizing. His own SaaSOvation walkthrough is presented as a confession: his first draft of the Scrum management model made exactly this mistake before he corrected it into `Product`, `BacklogItem`, and `Task` as three separate Aggregate roots, each referencing the others by identity. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s "God Aggregate" anti-pattern-table entry names the failure mode correctly but as a bare one-line description ("one Aggregate absorbs every related concept 'to be safe'") — it doesn't carry Vernon's specific warning that this is a *seductive*, well-intentioned mistake driven by a false sense of safety, which is exactly why it's worth a fuller worked example rather than a one-line anti-pattern table row.
- **Rule 3 (Reference Other Aggregates by Identity) has a subtlety the mechanical "store an ID, not a pointer" statement misses: the temptation to embed a direct reference is strongest precisely when the relationship is "true" in the domain (a real one-to-many or composition relationship), and Vernon's guidance is to separate the question "does the domain language say this object *has* these other objects" from the question "must this object be transactionally consistent with those other objects."** These are different questions with different answers surprisingly often — `Product` genuinely "has" `BacklogItem`s in the Ubiquitous Language (a domain expert would say so unprompted), but that linguistic/conceptual ownership does not imply the transactional consistency that would justify making `BacklogItem` unreachable except through `Product`. Vernon's explicit warning: a modeler who lets the *conceptual* "has-a" relationship drive the Aggregate boundary will systematically over-cluster, because natural language "ownership" is a much weaker and more common relationship than "must be atomically consistent with." `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s Rule 2 states the mechanics (store IDs, Go code sample showing `storageSourceID StorageSourceID` vs. `storageSource *StorageSource`) correctly but doesn't name *why* the temptation exists or that "the domain calls it 'has'" is specifically the wrong signal to trust.
- **Rule 3 also covers a performance-shaped mistake in the opposite direction: designing a root that must eagerly load large collections of children "for convenience," even when references are correctly by-ID.** Vernon's own example: a `Product` root that must load every `BacklogItem` ID it "contains" into an in-memory collection field just to answer "does this product have any backlog items" is itself a scaling hazard if the collection is unbounded, even though each individual `BacklogItem` is correctly its own Aggregate referenced by ID. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s Sizing-Under-Contention bullet "Unbounded collections force a split" already reaches the same conclusion independently, but frames it purely as a Rule-5 (small-Aggregates) concern rather than connecting it back to Rule 2/3's referencing discipline — in Vernon's own argument these are the same underlying mistake surfacing in two rules at once, and a modeler should recognize it as one failure mode, not two unrelated checklist items.
- **Rule 4 (Use Eventual Consistency Outside the Boundary) is Vernon's answer to "then how does anything outside the Aggregate ever find out its state changed, or get updated as a consequence?" — and his mechanism is explicitly Domain Events, published from inside the same transaction that changed the Aggregate's state, consumed asynchronously by whatever needs to react.** This is the direct structural payoff of Rules 1–3: because the Aggregate boundary is drawn tightly around only the true invariant, everything else that conceptually seems related (other Aggregates that should eventually reflect this change) is explicitly pushed outside the boundary and handled through eventual consistency rather than forced into the same transaction. Vernon frames this not as a compromise but as the *correct* design outcome — a system where every Aggregate is small and self-consistent, and cross-Aggregate effects propagate reliably but not instantly.
- **Vernon's explicit rule of thumb on scope: a single use case (a single client command/request) should, ideally, modify exactly one Aggregate instance within its own transaction.** Any additional Aggregates that logically need to change as a consequence of that same use case should be updated via eventual consistency — separate transactions, separate points in time, driven by the Domain Event the first transaction produced — rather than folded into the same atomic unit of work. He is not fully absolutist here (see the Caveats/exceptions concept below): he acknowledges that in some designs a use case may need to touch more than one Aggregate in one transaction, but treats this as the exception requiring justification, not the default shape to design toward. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s Rule 3 ("One Aggregate per Transaction") states this conclusion correctly and lists the three resolutions (reconsider the boundary / accept eventual consistency / use a Saga) — a faithful summary — but doesn't carry Vernon's framing that this is a *design goal to aim a use case at*, not merely a rule to check after the fact once the use case is already designed around multiple Aggregates.
- **Vernon gives concrete guidance on failure and retry handling for the eventual-consistency updates Rule 4 depends on, because a Domain-Event-driven update that silently fails leaves the system in a permanently inconsistent state — worse than the synchronous alternative it replaced.** His guidance: the mechanism that delivers a Domain Event to its consumer must be at-least-once and durable (the event must survive a crash between the originating Aggregate's commit and the consumer's processing), and the consumer's handling of that event must be idempotent, because at-least-once delivery means the same event may be processed more than once. He also discusses that the business process should be designed with the awareness that eventual consistency has a real (if usually short) window — a user-facing flow that depends on a second Aggregate's eventual update should communicate a "pending"/"processing" state rather than assume the second Aggregate is already updated the instant the first transaction commits. This durability-and-idempotency guidance is functionally identical to what this repo's own `skills/domain-event-catalog/SKILL.md` and `skills/go-event-publisher/SKILL.md` already implement (at-least-once via the outbox relay, idempotent consumers via `processed_events` dedup) — a case where the existing implementation independently arrived at exactly what Vernon prescribes, without ever citing him.
- **On reliably publishing the Domain Event in the first place, Vernon's own recommended mechanism is functionally the Transactional Outbox, described years before that exact term was popularized (the term is most commonly attributed to Chris Richardson's later microservices.io pattern catalog, not to Vernon).** Vernon's IDDD treatment of Domain Event publication (Ch. 8, "Domain Events," which sits alongside but is distinct from the Effective Aggregate Design chapter) discusses the atomicity problem directly: an Aggregate's state change and the publication of the resulting Domain Event must not be allowed to succeed independently of one another (either both happen or neither does), and his proposed resolution is to persist the event as part of the same local transaction that persists the Aggregate's state change, then have a separate, subsequent mechanism reliably forward the persisted event to the messaging infrastructure. This is the same shape as this repo's own `skills/go-repository-pattern/SKILL.md` (writes the outbox row in the same `tx` as the Aggregate's row) and `skills/go-event-publisher/SKILL.md` (the relay that polls and forwards) — a strong, faithful alignment worth stating explicitly, since neither skill currently attributes the pattern's lineage to Vernon, and a reader could otherwise assume "Transactional Outbox" is a purely microservices-era invention with no DDD-tactical-pattern pedigree.
- **Vernon separates two entirely different publication mechanisms that this repo's skills don't currently distinguish: an in-process, synchronous `DomainEventPublisher` for subscribers living in the same process/module as the Aggregate, versus the durable, asynchronous mechanism (the outbox-equivalent) for subscribers outside the process — including subscribers in other Bounded Contexts.** The in-process mechanism is a simple, synchronous, thread-local (in Vernon's Java framing) publish-subscribe registry invoked directly inside the Aggregate's own method, useful when a same-process collaborator (e.g., a same-context Application Service) needs to react to the event immediately without going through the durability machinery at all. This repo's default architecture (separate microservices per Bounded Context, communicating only via the broker) means the in-process variant is largely moot for cross-service concerns, but it remains directly relevant *within* a single service for same-process, same-transaction-adjacent reactions (e.g., updating an in-memory cache or triggering a synchronous same-service side effect) — a distinction neither `skills/go-domain-model/SKILL.md` nor `skills/go-event-publisher/SKILL.md` currently draws; both skills implicitly assume every Domain Event goes through the full outbox-and-relay path, even for effects that never need to leave the process.
- **Vernon's identity-generation guidance: prefer client-side (application-generated) identity over persistence-generated (database-surrogate) identity for Aggregate Roots, and his reasoning is about *when* a valid, complete instance can be said to exist, not merely a stylistic preference.** With a database-generated ID (an auto-increment/serial/identity column), the Aggregate does not have its real identity until *after* the initial `INSERT` returns — which means the object cannot be considered fully constructed and valid the moment its constructor runs, any Domain Event fired at creation time cannot correctly carry the new Aggregate's ID (because it doesn't exist yet), and unit tests that want to construct a fully valid instance are forced to either fake an ID or actually hit a database. With client-generated identity (Vernon's own preference, typically a UUID), the Aggregate is fully valid, ID included, the instant the constructor returns — before persistence has happened at all — which lets a creation-time Domain Event correctly reference the new Aggregate's real ID, and lets unit tests construct fully valid instances with zero database dependency. `skills/go-domain-model/SKILL.md`'s `NewDataAsset(id, tenantID, sourceID uuid.UUID, now time.Time)` already takes `id` as a caller-supplied parameter (client-generated) rather than generating it via a database sequence — this is a faithful, already-correct instance of Vernon's preferred approach, but the skill never states this is a deliberate identity-generation strategy choice, why it was made, or what the alternative (and its costs) would have been; a future reader could easily "fix" it into a DB-generated ID without realizing they'd be reintroducing exactly the problem Vernon's guidance exists to avoid.
- **Vernon's secondary technique for identity generation: when the ID-generation logic itself has real complexity (e.g., a human-readable, sequential, or business-meaningful identifier rather than an opaque UUID), delegate it to a `nextIdentity()`-style method on the Aggregate's own Repository, called by the client *before* constructing the Aggregate, rather than embedding generation logic inside the Aggregate constructor itself.** This keeps the Aggregate's constructor simple (it only accepts a valid, already-generated ID as one of its parameters, as `NewDataAsset` already does) while still keeping ID generation conceptually a domain-adjacent concern (owned by the Repository, which is domain-facing, not an infrastructure detail invisible to the domain). This repo's `skills/go-repository-pattern/SKILL.md` doesn't currently expose a `NextIdentity()`-style method on any repository — a minor, non-urgent gap, since plain `uuid.New()` calls at the point of construction are sufficient for opaque UUIDs and don't need this indirection; the technique becomes relevant only if this repo ever needs a business-meaningful (rather than opaque) Aggregate identifier.
- **On concurrency control for persisting Aggregates, Vernon states a clear default (optimistic) with an explicit, narrower escape hatch (pessimistic) — he is not absolutist about always using optimistic concurrency, but treats it as the correct default for the same reasons that motivate Rule 2 (small Aggregates, high throughput, low expected contention).** His reasoning for the default: if Aggregates are correctly sized per Rules 1–3, the number of concurrent writers contending for any single instance should be low, making the far cheaper optimistic approach (a version/timestamp check at write time, retry on conflict) preferable to pessimistic locking, which holds a database lock for the duration of a transaction (including any user "think time" if the transaction spans a request-response round trip) and directly limits throughput under load. His narrower exception: pessimistic locking (`SELECT ... FOR UPDATE`-style row locking) may still be justified for specific, narrow scenarios where the cost of a failed optimistic attempt is unacceptably high, or for batch/administrative processes (rather than interactive user-facing requests) where holding a lock briefly is safe and simpler than designing retry logic. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s "Sizing Under Contention" section and `skills/go-repository-pattern/SKILL.md`'s compare-and-swap `UPDATE ... WHERE ... AND version = $N` pattern already implement Vernon's *default* faithfully and in real depth, but neither skill mentions pessimistic locking as a legitimate, narrower alternative at all — as currently written, both skills read as if optimistic concurrency is the only option ever available, rather than the correct default with a documented, rare exception.
- **Vernon is explicitly not absolutist about any of the four rules — he states directly that they are guidelines for the common case, not universal laws, and gives his own criteria for when breaking one is the right engineering call.** His stated conditions for a deliberate exception: (a) a genuine business invariant really does span what would otherwise be two separate Aggregates (his own example category: financial transactions requiring literal atomicity, like a debit and its corresponding credit, where "eventual" consistency would be a real business defect, not just an engineering inconvenience) — in that case, the correct move is to accept the larger Aggregate rather than dogmatically split it merely because "small is the rule"; (b) measured, real performance data (not a hypothesis) shows that the ID-reference-plus-separate-query pattern from Rule 3 is a genuine, material bottleneck for a specific read-heavy path — in that narrow case, Vernon allows a read-only, explicitly-a-cache/denormalized copy of data sourced from another Aggregate, with the hard constraint that the copy is never authoritative and the write/modification path still goes exclusively through the owning Aggregate's own Repository. In both cases his emphasis is that the exception must be deliberate, documented, and driven by a real measured or business-mandated need — never a default reached for out of convenience or because splitting felt like more work. `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`'s own header states "These rules are non-negotiable. Violations produce a domain model that cannot enforce its own rules" — this is a materially stronger, more absolutist claim than Vernon himself ever makes about his own four rules, and is worth softening in the rebuild to match the source's own explicitly-not-absolutist stance, while still keeping the bar for an exception high (documented, deliberate, justified by a real invariant or measured performance need — not "it seemed easier").
- **Vernon's treatment of testability as a *consequence* of correct Aggregate design, not a separate concern: small Aggregates with client-generated identity and no eager cross-Aggregate loading are trivially unit-testable in complete isolation, with zero database or object-graph setup cost.** He presents this as a signal, not just a benefit — if constructing a valid instance of an Aggregate for a unit test requires an elaborate object graph, a database round trip for an ID, or stubbing out several other Aggregates' data, that difficulty is itself diagnostic evidence the boundary is drawn wrong (too large, or ID generation delegated to the wrong place), rather than merely an inconvenience to route around with more test helpers. This repo's own `skills/go-domain-model/SKILL.md` Output Format ("table-driven invariant tests — written first") already benefits from this property because of its client-generated-ID, small-Aggregate design — but neither skill states that *testability itself* is one of the diagnostic signals a modeler should use to sanity-check whether an Aggregate boundary is correctly drawn in the first place.

## Actionable Techniques

- For every candidate invariant, apply Vernon's three-question test explicitly and in writing before drawing the boundary around it: would a momentary violation cause real business harm (not just surprise)? Would fixing a violation after the fact require more than a reasonable compensating action? Is the rule already enforced by an external system such that atomic re-enforcement here is redundant? Only a rule that fails all three escape hatches (real harm, no easy compensation, not already enforced elsewhere) earns a place inside the consistency boundary.
- Before finalizing any Aggregate that "has" or "contains" other objects in the domain language, explicitly separate the conceptual-ownership question ("does the Ubiquitous Language say this object has these others?") from the transactional-consistency question ("must this object be atomically consistent with those others at every instant?") — write both answers down; a "yes/no" mismatch (has-a is true, atomic-consistency is false) is the single most common signal of an oversized Aggregate candidate.
- When sizing an Aggregate, evaluate all three of Vernon's angles separately rather than one blended "small is good" instinct: name the performance cost of loading the full object graph, the scalability cost of the per-instance lock/version under expected concurrent load, and the collaboration cost of how many distinct legitimate user actions would contend for the same instance if it stayed this size.
- For every Aggregate Root, record the identity-generation strategy explicitly (client-generated via UUID at construction, vs. persistence-generated via DB sequence, vs. Repository `nextIdentity()` for business-meaningful IDs) and justify the choice against Vernon's own criterion: can a fully valid instance, including its final ID, be constructed before the first database write, with zero I/O, in a unit test? If not, state why this Aggregate is a deliberate exception.
- For every Aggregate Root's persistence, state the concurrency strategy explicitly (optimistic version-field default, or pessimistic row lock as a rare, justified exception) and require the exception case to name the specific narrow condition that justifies it (batch/administrative context, or a cost-of-failed-optimistic-attempt argument) rather than defaulting to pessimistic out of unfamiliarity with retry handling.
- Whenever a use case's design is drafted, count how many distinct Aggregate instances it modifies within one transaction; treat any count above one as requiring an explicit justification (a genuine cross-object true invariant per the exception criteria) or a redesign toward Rule 4 (one Aggregate synchronously, the rest via a published Domain Event and eventual consistency).
- For every cross-Aggregate eventual-consistency update, verify both halves of Vernon's reliability requirement are actually in place: the publishing side is durable and at-least-once (crash-safe between the source Aggregate's commit and the event reaching the consumer), and the consuming side is idempotent (safe to process the same event more than once) — treat either half being missing as a defect, not a future improvement.
- When a rule genuinely seems to require breaking one of the four Aggregate design rules, run Vernon's own exception checklist before proceeding: is this a real, business-mandated atomic invariant (not just an engineering convenience), or is this a *measured* (not hypothesized) performance bottleneck in a specific read path? Document which one, and if it's the performance case, constrain the exception to a read-only denormalized copy that is never the write path's system of record.
- Use ease-of-unit-testing as a live diagnostic during Aggregate design, not just a downstream benefit: if constructing a valid instance for a test requires a database round trip, an elaborate object graph, or stubbed data from other Aggregates, treat that friction as evidence the boundary (or the identity-generation strategy) is drawn wrong, and revisit Rules 1–3 before adding more test scaffolding to work around it.

## Key Terminology

- **Consistency Boundary** — Vernon's precise name for the exact scope within which a true invariant is atomically enforced; distinct from `Bounded Context` (a linguistic/team/model scope) and narrower than "Aggregate" as a bare structural term — the boundary is defined by the invariant, not the other way around. Worth adding to `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` alongside `Aggregate` and `Aggregate Root`, which the glossary currently defines structurally but without this precise "the boundary is the invariant's scope, nothing more" framing.
- **True Invariant** — a business rule that must hold at every instant, has no acceptable compensating fix-up if briefly violated, and is not already enforced by an external system — Vernon's specific, narrower sense of "invariant" as distinguished from a business rule that merely looks urgent. Candidate glossary addition, distinguished explicitly from the bare word "invariant" already used informally throughout `skills/aggregate-design/SKILL.md`.
- **Client-Generated Identity** (also Application-Generated Identity) — an Aggregate Root's identity assigned by application code (typically a UUID) at construction time, before any persistence I/O, so a fully valid instance — ID included — exists prior to the first database write. Vernon's stated preference over persistence-generated identity.
- **Persistence-Generated Identity** (Surrogate Identity) — an Aggregate Root's identity assigned by the database itself (an auto-increment/serial/identity column) only after the first successful insert, meaning no fully valid instance exists, ID included, until after that round trip. Vernon's disfavored default, kept only where a specific reason (e.g., interop with a legacy system expecting sequential IDs) requires it.
- **`nextIdentity()`** — Vernon's named technique for delegating identity generation to a method on the Aggregate's own Repository, called before construction, reserved for identifiers complex enough (business-meaningful, sequential, human-readable) that generation doesn't belong inline in the constructor, while still keeping the concern domain-facing rather than a hidden infrastructure detail.
- **Optimistic Concurrency** *(already implemented throughout this repo's skills but not previously attributed to this book as a primary source)* — a concurrency-control strategy that checks a version/timestamp at write time and rejects (rather than blocks) a conflicting concurrent write, relying on retries being rare because Aggregates are correctly small; Vernon's stated default.
- **Pessimistic Concurrency** — a concurrency-control strategy that acquires an exclusive lock on an Aggregate's row(s) for the duration of a transaction, blocking other writers rather than rejecting them after the fact; Vernon's narrow, justified exception to the optimistic default, reserved for high-failure-cost or batch/administrative scenarios.
- **Has-a vs. Must-Be-Atomic-With** — the specific diagnostic distinction (not a single named term in the book, but a recurring, explicit contrast Vernon draws) between a domain-language ownership relationship ("Product has BacklogItems") and a transactional-consistency requirement — the first does not imply the second, and conflating them is Vernon's named root cause of oversized Aggregates.

## Caveats

- **Confidence level, overall:** I am highly confident in this book's headline content — the four numbered rules, the SaaSOvation Product/BacklogItem/Task worked example and its "one big Aggregate" correction narrative, the client-generated-identity preference and its rationale, the optimistic-concurrency-as-default-with-pessimistic-exception guidance, and the explicit not-absolutist framing around all four rules. These are the most widely cited, specifically-Vernon (as opposed to generically-DDD) contributions in the modern DDD literature, closely tracking the original three-part "Effective Aggregate Design" article this book folded in. I am **less confident on exact chapter/section numbers** — IDDD's chapter organization differs somewhat across printings, and the Effective Aggregate Design material's boundary with the separate Domain Events chapter (Ch. 8 in most editions) and the concurrency/repository material (which overlaps with an Aggregates-and-Repositories-adjacent chapter) is best treated as an approximate signpost, not a verified page citation.
- **The Transactional Outbox provenance claim in this document is a reasoned attribution, not a verified direct quotation.** Vernon's IDDD does discuss persisting a Domain Event durably in the same local transaction as the Aggregate's state change and forwarding it via a separate mechanism — functionally identical to the pattern later popularized under the "Transactional Outbox" name (most commonly credited to Chris Richardson's microservices.io catalog) — but Vernon's book does not use that exact term. Treat the alignment as a substantive match worth noting for provenance purposes, not as evidence Vernon coined the phrase.
- **IDDD's worked examples are Java-centric, written against a Hibernate/JPA-and-session-based persistence idiom, much like Evans' own text.** The `nextIdentity()`-on-Repository pattern, the in-process synchronous `DomainEventPublisher`, and the identity-generation discussion all assume an ORM-and-session-based stack quite different from this repo's raw-SQL-via-pgx approach; the Go adaptations proposed here (UUID-at-construction, the outbox relay, a CAS-based `UPDATE`) are this document's own extrapolation of Vernon's *principles* onto this repo's stack, not literal ports of his code samples.
- **The SaaSOvation Scrum-management domain (Product/BacklogItem/Release/Sprint/Task) is Vernon's own single running example across most of the book, not a generic case study — its specifics don't map onto this repo's data-estate/compliance domain.** The reasoning it illustrates (has-a vs. must-be-atomic-with, the seductive false-safety of one big Aggregate) generalizes directly, but this document's mapping of that reasoning onto `DataAsset`/`StorageSource`/`ExtractedEntity` is this document's own extrapolation, consistent with but not identical to Vernon's own worked walkthrough.
- **This book is explicitly more prescriptive and rule-based than Evans' foundational, looser treatment — that difference in register is itself part of what this research file is capturing, not a sign one book is more "correct" than the other.** Where Evans states a principle and trusts the reader to apply judgment, Vernon converts the same principle into an explicit, sequenced, checkable procedure with named anti-patterns and a stated exception process. Both are faithful to the same underlying DDD tactical-design tradition; a future skill rebuild drawing on both should credit Vernon specifically for the four-rule structure, the identity-generation and concurrency guidance, and the not-absolutist exception criteria — content that is distinctly his contribution, not a restatement of Evans.
- **This document deliberately does not re-derive Entity/Value Object/Repository/Factory content already covered in `research/domain-driven-design/domain-driven-design-evans.md`**, per this research assignment's explicit scope instruction — Vernon's own treatment of those patterns in IDDD (Ch. 5–6 in most editions) is broadly consistent with Evans' original text and adds comparatively little beyond what book 1 already covers; this document's scope is limited to what is distinctly Vernon's own prescriptive contribution to Aggregate design specifically.
