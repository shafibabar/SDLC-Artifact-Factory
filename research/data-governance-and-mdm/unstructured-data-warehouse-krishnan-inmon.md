# Building the Unstructured Data Warehouse: Architecture, Analysis, and Design
**Authors:** Krish Krishnan and W.H. Inmon
**Domain:** Data Governance and Master Data Management

## Core Concepts

- **Textual ETL: the discipline of turning free text into structured, queryable facts — and this repo's pipeline currently has no modeled stage that does this, only a stage that appears to do it invisibly.** Inmon's signature contribution (coined and popularized across this and his companion texts) is that unstructured content cannot go straight from "raw bytes" to "business insight" — it must pass through an explicit textual ETL process: **parse** the raw format into text plus structural context (pages, paragraphs, headings, tables, cells), **tag/extract** entities, facts, dates, amounts, and relationships out of that text using NLP techniques (tokenization, stemming, named-entity recognition, taxonomy-driven categorization, disambiguation), and **load** the result into structured tables a BI tool or downstream analytic process can query like any other fact table. This repo's `skills/data-pipeline-implementation/SKILL.md` (read in full) has an Entity Extraction stage worker whose `extractEntities` function dispatches by file type into `run.Units` (`pdfPages`, `docxParagraphs`, `xlsxSheets`) and calls a single opaque `extractUnit(ctx, unit)` per unit, checkpointed page-by-page — but nowhere in that skill, or in `skills/data-classification/SKILL.md`, or in `skills/data-model-design/SKILL.md`, is there a named, modeled distinction between the **parse** step (turn PDF bytes into text + layout — a genuinely hard, format-specific problem: PDF text extraction differs completely from DOCX XML-paragraph walking, which differs completely from XLSX cell/sheet iteration) and the **tag/extract** step (find a PERSON_NAME or SSN inside that already-parsed text). `extractUnit` is a black box that appears to do both in one undifferentiated call, with no intermediate, modeled artifact for "the parsed, structured representation of this document's content" — the pipeline goes directly from `FileProcessed` to `EntityExtracted` with nothing in between to inspect, reuse, or re-derive facts from without re-parsing the original file. This is precisely the gap the task brief anticipated: the pipeline "jumps straight from 'file exists' to 'classified' with no modeled extraction/structuring stage," and having read every relevant skill, that description is accurate — there is no textual ETL layer, only its two endpoints.
- **A document's structure (sections, headings, tables, paragraphs) is analytically valuable content in its own right — and this repo throws it away at the first opportunity.** Krishnan & Inmon devote real attention to the fact that unstructured content is not just "a bag of words" — a table inside a DOCX or XLSX has rows, columns, and headers that carry meaning no linear-text NER pass can recover once flattened (a column labeled `SSN` next to a column of nine-digit strings is a much stronger and cheaper structural signal than running entity detection on each cell in isolation), and a heading hierarchy ("Section 4: Compensation" containing a salary figure) changes what that figure *means* semantically. `data-pipeline-implementation`'s worked example dispatches `xlsxSheets` as its checkpoint granularity and `docxParagraphs` for DOCX — both already have the structural boundary available at parse time (a sheet has columns and headers; a DOCX paragraph sits inside a heading hierarchy) — but nothing downstream (`extracted_entities`' schema in `data-model-design`: `entity_type`, `confidence`, `location JSONB`) captures or reuses that structure. `location` is described only as "page/offset, queried as a whole" — a position, not a structural context. The document's own shape, which the parser necessarily has in hand for one moment during parsing, is discarded before the entity extraction result is ever persisted.
- **Content-derived metadata is a distinct, richer output than the compliance-entity code list `data-classification` already extracts, and conflating the two under-serves both.** `data-classification`'s detected-entity-type table (`EMAIL, PHONE, PERSON_NAME, SSN, NATIONAL_ID, PASSPORT, HEALTH_TERM, DIAGNOSIS, ACCOUNT_NUMBER, IBAN`) is a **narrow, compliance-driven** entity list — every entry exists because it drives a sensitivity level. Krishnan & Inmon's textual ETL vocabulary is broader: dates, organization names, monetary amounts, topics/categories (taxonomy-driven classification of *what a document is about*, not just what regulated data it contains), and relationships between extracted facts (e.g., "Person X received Amount Y on Date Z" as a linked fact, not three independent entity rows). This repo's product may reasonably choose not to need topic/sentiment analysis for its MVP compliance use case — but the *architecture* question of whether entity extraction is meant to be a general-purpose textual ETL capability (extensible to new fact types later) or a permanently compliance-only detector is never asked. Today's schema (`extracted_entities` with a fixed `entity_type` enum implied by `data-classification`'s table) reads as the latter by default, not by decision.
- **Textual disambiguation — the same word or string meaning different things depending on context — is a distinct data-quality problem the book names precisely, and this repo's confidence-threshold model doesn't have a slot for it.** Krishnan & Inmon flag that free text is systematically ambiguous in ways structured data is not: "Washington" could be a person's surname, a US state, or an organization; a nine-digit number could be an SSN, an account number, or a zip+4 with a typo. `skills/data-quality-rules/SKILL.md`'s Accuracy dimension ("Confidence-scored comparison against ground truth or a trusted source") and its confidence-threshold table (read in full: EMAIL/PHONE/PERSON_NAME ≥ 0.85, SSN/NATIONAL_ID/PASSPORT ≥ 0.90) treats every below-threshold detection as a single kind of problem — "not confident enough yet" — with one resolution path (quarantine → steward review). Disambiguation is a *different* failure mode: the extractor may be highly confident it found a 9-digit number and highly confident that shape usually means SSN, while the actual answer depends on document context (is this a payroll document or a shipping manifest?) that a pattern-match confidence score cannot express. Nothing in `data-quality-rules` or `data-classification` distinguishes "I am not sure this is real" (the existing confidence-threshold case) from "I am confident I found *something*, but not confident *what kind* of thing it is" (disambiguation) — both currently collapse into the same quarantine queue with the same review UI, when a human reviewer resolving each is answering a genuinely different question.
- **OCR-derived text has its own, format-specific quality profile that this repo names once, in passing, and never designs for.** `data-pipeline-implementation`'s Python Exception section (read in full) mentions "an OCR fallback worker" as a justified non-Go exception ("uses <ocr-library>, no equivalent Go binding exists") — this is the only place in the entire repo that acknowledges scanned/image-based documents exist as an input shape at all (`data-classification`'s own worked example is explicitly "A scanned (image-based) PDF" in `data-quality-rules`). Krishnan & Inmon treat OCR quality as its own axis distinct from NER confidence: OCR introduces character-level noise (a scanned "5" misread as "S", a smudge splitting one word into two) *before* any entity detection runs on the resulting text, so a low downstream entity-confidence score can have two very different root causes — the NER model is unsure, or the OCR transcription it's reading is simply wrong. `data-quality-rules`' worked example ("Entity 3: SSN masked, confidence 0.62") never asks which of those two causes produced the low score, and the two would call for different remediations (re-run at higher OCR resolution/DPI vs. tune the NER threshold) that this repo's single confidence number cannot distinguish between.
- **An unstructured data warehouse's layered architecture (Raw/Ingest → Parsed/Structured Content → Integrated → Presentation) is the book's organizing model, and this repo's pipeline topology is a partial, unlabeled instance of it.** Krishnan & Inmon describe the unstructured counterpart to Inmon's own classic structured-DW layering: a Raw layer holding the original content untouched, a Text/Content-processing layer where textual ETL produces structured facts and metadata, an Integration layer where those facts are combined with the organization's existing structured data (customer records, transaction history), and a Presentation layer for BI/reporting. `data-pipeline-design`'s topology (`FileDiscovered → FileProcessed → EntityExtracted → {Graph Update, Classification, Compliance Rule Engine}`) genuinely covers Raw (implicitly, at customer storage — "without raw content ever leaving customer infrastructure") and something like a Text-processing layer (Entity Extraction) — but it was never named or designed as an instance of this reference architecture, so the Integration layer (combining `extracted_entities` with any structured data the product ever ingests) and a genuine Presentation layer (beyond `dashboard-specification`'s widget-level queries, already flagged as too narrow by `research/data-governance-and-mdm/data-as-a-product-gioia.md`) have no designed home either.
- **Indexing and search strategy for unstructured content is a first-class technique in this book, and this repo has exactly one row of a table for it.** Krishnan & Inmon spend real space on how unstructured content is made findable at scale: inverted indexes over extracted text, taxonomy-driven faceted search (search by content-derived category, not just full-text match), and combining structured metadata (sensitivity level, entity type, source) with unstructured full-text relevance in one query surface. `skills/data-model-design/SKILL.md`'s Polyglot Persistence table (read in full) has exactly one relevant row: `Elasticsearch | Full-text search, ranking, alert storage with flexible querying | ... | Document full-text index, compliance alert search`. That single row is the entire indexing/search design in this repo — there is no discussion of what gets indexed (raw text? only entity mentions? both?), how faceted/combined structured+unstructured queries are meant to work (e.g., "find all Restricted documents mentioning 'severance' in the Legal data source"), or a chunking/segmentation strategy for indexing long documents (a whole-document index vs. a page/paragraph-level index changes both relevance quality and what a search result can highlight).

## Actionable Techniques

- Before extending `data-pipeline-implementation`'s `extractUnit` any further, split it explicitly into two named steps with two named outputs: a **parse** step producing a structured content representation (text plus positional/structural context — page, paragraph, heading path, table cell coordinates) as its own artifact, and a **tag/extract** step consuming that representation to produce `extracted_entities` rows. Today both are fused inside one opaque function call with no intermediate artifact to inspect or reuse.
- When a parsed unit has inherent structure (an XLSX sheet's column headers, a DOCX heading hierarchy above a paragraph), carry that structure forward into the extraction step rather than discarding it after parsing — a column header adjacent to a candidate SSN pattern is free, high-value evidence that today's flat `location JSONB` (page/offset only) cannot express and today's extractor cannot use.
- Decide explicitly, as a recorded architecture decision, whether entity extraction is scoped permanently to `data-classification`'s compliance entity list, or is meant to be a general textual-ETL capability (dates, organizations, amounts, topics, linked facts) that compliance entities are a subset of — this determines whether `extracted_entities`' schema should stay a fixed compliance-entity enum or be designed for extensibility now, before a second, non-compliance use case forces a painful migration.
- Add a distinct outcome to the quality-gate/quarantine model for **disambiguation** (confident a pattern was found, unsure what kind of thing it is) as opposed to **low confidence** (unsure the pattern is real at all) — these are different judgments a steward makes differently, and today both land in the same quarantine queue under the same confidence-threshold mechanism.
- Track OCR transcription quality (e.g., an OCR engine's own per-page confidence or character-error-rate signal) as a distinct upstream quality dimension from NER/entity-detection confidence, so a low downstream entity-confidence score can be attributed to "the OCR text itself is unreliable here" versus "the entity detector is unsure" — these have different remediations (higher-DPI rescan vs. threshold tuning) that a single blended confidence number currently cannot distinguish.
- Name and document this repo's own pipeline topology against Krishnan & Inmon's four-layer reference architecture (Raw / Text-processing / Integration / Presentation) explicitly — even briefly — so that the currently-undesigned Integration layer (combining `extracted_entities` with any structured data the product later ingests) is recognized as a deliberate gap to fill later, not an accident of scope.
- Before this product's first customer-facing search or "find documents mentioning X" feature ships, design the indexing strategy beyond the single Elasticsearch table row: decide the indexing granularity (whole-document vs. page/paragraph-level), whether the index carries only entity mentions or full text, and how a query combines structured facets (`sensitivity_level`, `entity_type`, `tenant_id`) with full-text relevance in one request.

## Candidate New Skills/Agents

- **`textual-etl-design` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`, implemented by `data-pipeline-implementation`'s Entity Extraction worker).** Teaches the book's core parse → tag/extract → load model for turning raw unstructured content into structured, queryable facts: format-specific parsing strategy per file type (PDF text+layout extraction, DOCX paragraph/heading walking, XLSX sheet/cell iteration — already dispatched by file type in `data-pipeline-implementation` but never named as a distinct "parse" concern), a modeled intermediate parsed-content representation carrying structural context (heading path, table cell coordinates) forward rather than discarding it, textual disambiguation as a distinct data-quality outcome from low-confidence detection, and an explicit decision about whether entity extraction generalizes beyond `data-classification`'s fixed compliance code list. Justification: this is the single most consequential gap this cluster has found relative to the product's actual subject matter — the entire pipeline currently treats "parse this document" and "find compliance entities in it" as one undifferentiated black-box call (`extractUnit`), with no artifact in between, no reuse of document structure, and no vocabulary for the specific failure modes (disambiguation, OCR-vs-NER confidence conflation) free text introduces that structured data never has. This is distinct from DAMA-DMBOK's already-proposed `document-content-lifecycle` (scoped to content-type taxonomy, near-duplicate detection, and format-obsolescence risk — lifecycle/retention concerns about the document as a whole) — this skill is scoped to the parsing/extraction *mechanics* that turn document bytes into structured facts in the first place, a mechanically prior and completely non-overlapping concern.
- **`unstructured-content-indexing-design` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`, consumed by `frontend-engineer`/`backend-engineer` for any search-facing feature).** Teaches indexing and search strategy for unstructured content specifically: inverted-index design over extracted text, indexing granularity decisions (whole-document vs. page/paragraph-level, which determines both relevance quality and what a search result can highlight), taxonomy-driven faceted search, and combining structured facets (`sensitivity_level`, `entity_type`, `tenant_id`) with full-text relevance in a single query surface. Justification: `data-model-design`'s Polyglot Persistence table gives Elasticsearch exactly one row ("Document full-text index, compliance alert search") with zero design behind it — this is a real, buildable technique this book devotes substantial space to, and this product's whole value proposition ("where does customer PII actually live," per the existing `user-persona` Maya Chen worked example already cited in `research/data-governance-and-mdm/simple-data-mdm-srinivasan.md`) is fundamentally a search-and-discovery problem over unstructured content that currently has no designed search layer at all.
- **`unstructured-structured-analytics-integration` (Skill, owned by `data-engineer`, consumed by `dashboard-specification`/`reporting-spec`; a companion to, not a duplicate of, `research/data-and-analytics/data-warehouse-toolkit-kimball-ross.md`'s already-proposed `dimensional-data-modeling`).** Teaches the book's Integration-layer technique: combining textual-ETL-derived facts (extracted entities, content-derived metadata) with any structured data the product ingests (source-system metadata, tenant/organization attributes, future customer-provided structured records) into a single analyzable layer — e.g., correlating extracted-PII density with data-source type or tenant industry for a compliance trend report. ## Key Terminology

Candidates for `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` — cross-reference the four prior files in this cluster first: **Master Data**, **Reference Data**, **Golden Record**, **Survivorship**, **Identity Resolution**, **Data Domain**, **Data Product**, **Data Contract**, **Decision Explainability**, and the rest of that cluster's already-proposed terms should not be redefined here, only cited. None of the terms below currently appear in the glossary, despite `data-pipeline-implementation` and `data-classification` already doing work these terms would precisely name:

- **Textual ETL** — the process of extracting structured facts, entities, and relationships from unstructured or semi-structured text and loading them into a structured, queryable form — distinct from conventional ETL, whose source data is already structured. `data-pipeline-implementation`'s Entity Extraction stage performs a version of this today without the term ever appearing.
- **Content Parsing** — the step of converting a raw file format (PDF, DOCX, XLSX) into text plus its structural context (pages, paragraphs, headings, tables), prior to and distinct from entity/fact extraction over that parsed content. Not currently named anywhere despite being dispatched by file type inside `data-pipeline-implementation`'s `extractEntities`.
- **Document Structure** — the hierarchical and tabular organization of a document's content (heading hierarchy, section nesting, table rows/columns/headers) that carries semantic meaning independent of the raw text itself, and which a parser has available but this repo currently discards before persisting extraction results.
- **Textual Disambiguation** — resolving which of several possible meanings an ambiguous extracted string or pattern actually has, given surrounding context (a name that could be a person or a place; a numeric pattern that could be several kinds of identifier) — distinct from low-confidence detection, which questions whether a pattern is real at all rather than what kind of thing it is.
- **Unstructured Data Warehouse** — an architecture (per Krishnan & Inmon, extending Inmon's classic structured data warehouse model) with a layered structure of Raw/Ingest, Text/Content-processing (where textual ETL happens), Integration (combining unstructured-derived facts with structured data), and Presentation — as distinct from a conventional structured-data warehouse's simpler source-to-target layering.
- **Content-Derived Metadata** — metadata about a document generated by analyzing its content (extracted topics, entities, dates, amounts) as opposed to file-system metadata (filename, size, modified date) or classification metadata (sensitivity level) — a category this repo's `data-classification` and `data-lineage-design` skills each touch a slice of but never name as a unified concept.
- **Faceted Search** — a search technique combining structured filter criteria (facets — e.g., sensitivity level, entity type, tenant, source) with unstructured full-text relevance ranking in a single query, as distinct from either pure structured filtering (SQL `WHERE`) or pure full-text search alone.

## Caveats

- **This book predates modern transformer-based NLP and large-language-model extraction techniques — its "textual ETL" mechanics (rule-based tagging, taxonomy-driven classification, statistical disambiguation) describe an earlier generation of NLP tooling.** The *architectural* insight — that unstructured content needs an explicit parse/tag-extract/load pipeline with a real, inspectable intermediate artifact, and that document structure and content-derived metadata are valuable, discardable-by-default outputs — is durable and exactly what this repo is missing. The specific extraction *techniques* the book describes (classical NER, taxonomy ontologies) should be treated as one valid implementation choice among several a future `textual-etl-design` skill would need to weigh against modern LLM-based extraction, not as a technology mandate.
- **Krishnan & Inmon's "unstructured data warehouse" assumes a genuine analytical back-room warehouse (the same pre-cloud, appliance-era assumption `research/data-and-analytics/data-warehouse-toolkit-kimball-ross.md`'s Caveats already flagged for Kimball & Ross).** This repo's frugal, PostgreSQL/Apache AGE/Redpanda stack has no separate physical warehouse and should not grow one on this book's authority alone — the *techniques* (textual ETL, indexing strategy, structured/unstructured integration) apply directly to this repo's existing pipeline and read-model layer; the book's *deployment* assumptions (a dedicated unstructured-DW appliance) do not.
- **The disambiguation and OCR-quality gaps identified here are real but not yet urgent relative to this product's MVP scope.** `data-quality-rules`' existing confidence-threshold-and-quarantine mechanism is a working, sound stopgap; splitting disambiguation and OCR-quality into distinct signals is valuable primarily once the steward review queue is large enough that conflating the two starts costing real reviewer time misdiagnosing root causes — sequence this the same way this cluster's other files have sequenced non-urgent depth (build it when the pain is first felt, not preemptively).
- **This is the most directly relevant book in the entire seven-title cluster to this specific product's technical surface, and that raises the stakes of getting the scope decision right rather than building broadly.** The strongest single recommendation from this file is the explicit parse/tag-extract split inside `data-pipeline-implementation` and the scope decision (compliance-only entity extraction vs. general textual-ETL capability) — both are cheap to decide now and expensive to retrofit once `extracted_entities`' schema and the pipeline's contracts have more consumers depending on today's undifferentiated shape.
- **Confidence level:** I am confident in this book's headline structure — the textual ETL concept (parse/tag-extract/load), the layered unstructured-data-warehouse architecture, the distinction between content-derived and file-system metadata, and indexing/search as a named technique for unstructured content — these are the book's well-established, frequently cited contributions, and Krishnan & Inmon (with Inmon as father of the structured data warehouse) are a primary, authoritative source for this specific extension of that thinking to unstructured content. I am **less confident on exact chapter structure and terminology**, which are my best approximation of the book's organization, not verified against the physical text page-by-page.
