# Simple Data: Master Data Management for People, Not Just Systems
**Authors:** Ram Srinivasan
**Domain:** Data Governance and Master Data Management

## Core Concepts

- **MDM succeeds or fails on whether business people *trust* the result, not on whether the matching algorithm is correct — a distinction none of this repo's data skills currently draw.** Srinivasan's central, repeated claim is that a technically flawless Golden Record that business users don't believe in gets quietly ignored — they keep their own spreadsheet "just in case," and the MDM investment produces a second, unused system of record. `skills/canonical-data-model/SKILL.md`'s `match_decisions` table and survivorship rules (read in full) are genuinely sound MDM mechanics — reversible, deterministic, tenant-scoped — but the skill's entire vocabulary is addressed to an engineer or architect: `method`, `score`, `retracted_at`, "deterministic termination," "highest-sensitivity-wins." Nowhere does it, or `skills/data-classification/SKILL.md`, or `skills/data-quality-rules/SKILL.md`, ask the different question Srinivasan opens the book with: if a real person looked at this specific decision, would they believe it, or would they quietly distrust the product and keep checking the source documents themselves? For a compliance product whose entire pitch is "trust our estate map instead of your team's folklore" (per `skills/user-persona/SKILL.md`'s Maya Chen persona: "cannot answer 'where does customer PII actually live?' with evidence — only with folklore"), this is not a soft concern — a customer who doesn't trust *why* the product classified or merged something will revert to folklore, which is the exact failure state the product exists to fix.
- **Data quality, explained to the person who has to act on it, needs a different vocabulary than the six DMBOK-style dimensions this repo already uses precisely.** `skills/data-quality-rules/SKILL.md` is engineered exactly right for its audience — completeness, accuracy, consistency, timeliness, validity, uniqueness, each with a `CHECK` constraint or pipeline-stage rule — but every worked example in that skill (the confidence-band routing table, the SQL constraints) is written for a data-engineer, not for the Compliance Officer who will eventually read a compliance report built on top of these gates. Srinivasan's plain-language reframing of the same dimensions — "is it here," "is it right," "is it the same everywhere," "is it current," "does it look real," "is it counted once" — is not a dumbed-down version of the DAMA taxonomy; it is the translation layer this repo has never built between its engineering-precise DQ rules and the non-technical stakeholder (`user-persona`'s Maya Chen is explicitly "technical literacy 3/5 — will not run CLI tools or read API docs") who has to act on a compliance finding that a DQ gate silently shaped.
- **"Start small, prove value, expand later" is a sequencing philosophy this repo's MDM design has never applied to itself — it built all three master entities as a single unit from the start.** `canonical-data-model`'s Master Data Management section introduces `Person`, `DataSource`, and `Organisation` together, with one shared matching/survivorship section, as though the natural unit of MDM work is "all master entities, all at once." Srinivasan's pragmatic, lean-MDM chapters argue the opposite for exactly this repo's situation (a small team, a first product, not yet a proven MDM program): pick the single highest-pain master entity, get its Golden Record trusted and adopted, and only then extend to the next. This is a genuinely different question from Loshin's `mdm-architecture-style-selection` (which style does *this* entity use) or Cervo & Allen's `mdm-hub-shared-infrastructure-design` (do entities share one mechanism) — it is about *sequencing effort*, not architecture, and neither of those two already-proposed candidates asks it.
- **Business users resist a single source of truth for identifiable, nameable reasons — loss of control and fear of being blamed — and this repo's steward/override mechanics are built to handle the *mechanics* of resistance without ever naming the *reason* for it.** `data-classification`'s "Manual classification wins and is audited" and `canonical-data-model`'s reversible `match_decisions` are both, in effect, already trust-repair mechanisms — a steward can override a wrong automated decision, and a bad merge can be un-done. But nowhere is it stated *why* these mechanisms exist from a people perspective: a steward who cannot override an automated decision stops trusting the system and starts working around it; a golden record that cannot be un-merged makes every steward afraid to trust the merge step at all, so they re-verify everything manually regardless of what the system says. Srinivasan's contribution is naming this causal chain explicitly — override/reversal isn't just an audit-safety feature, it's the mechanism that makes adoption possible at all.
- **`user-persona`'s and `stakeholder-mapping`'s existing coverage is aimed at the product's external customer relationship (who buys, who champions, who blocks the sale) — neither addresses trust in the product's own automated data decisions as a distinct persona dimension, even though the same personas are the ones who will read compliance reports built on classification and match decisions.** `stakeholder-mapping`'s worked example (read in full) places the CISO as the highest-risk Blocker and mitigates that risk with "a security architecture brief showing zero data egress and mTLS-by-default" — a security-trust argument, not a data-decision-trust argument. `user-persona`'s Required Persona Attributes list (Identity Anchors, Motivations and Goals, Frustrations and Blockers, Behaviours, Relationship to Product) has no attribute capturing whether a persona needs to *understand and verify* an automated decision before acting on it, versus accepting it on faith — a materially different adoption barrier from the ones already listed ("anything that requires her to file an engineering ticket," per Maya Chen's worked example) and one Srinivasan treats as a first-class MDM adoption risk.
- **`data-storytelling` already solves an adjacent but distinct problem — presenting an *analytical trend* to a stakeholder — and its existence is worth naming precisely so a future skill doesn't duplicate it.** `skills/data-storytelling/SKILL.md`'s Context→Insight→Recommendation→Call-to-Action structure and its worked example ("Extraction-Confidence Trend, Presented to Shafi") are addressed to Shafi, internally, about a *pattern over time* (a declining confidence trend). Srinivasan's decision-trust angle is about a different moment and a different audience entirely: a single, specific automated decision (this document is Restricted; these two records are the same Person) explained at the point an external, non-technical customer encounters it in the product — not a retrospective trend narrative to the internal operator. The two are complementary, not overlapping: one explains "what changed and what should we do," the other explains "why did the system decide this, and can I trust it."

## Actionable Techniques

- Before extending `canonical-data-model`'s Master Data Management section to a fourth master entity, apply Srinivasan's sequencing test: has the *current* highest-pain entity's Golden Record actually been trusted and adopted (by whichever persona consumes it), or is the repo about to add breadth before depth is proven? This is a different question from Loshin's per-entity style choice or Cervo & Allen's shared-infrastructure decision — it asks whether now is even the right time to add another entity at all.
- Whenever a future skill defines an automated decision with real consequences for a non-technical stakeholder (a classification level, a match/merge, a quality-gate outcome), pair the mechanism with a plain-language explanation template: what happened, in one sentence a Compliance-Officer-literacy reader can act on without a glossary — e.g., "this document was marked Restricted because it contains what looks like a Social Security Number, detected with high confidence" rather than exposing `entity_type=SSN, confidence=0.94, propagation_rule=max`.
- When translating any of `data-quality-rules`' six dimensions for a customer-facing artifact (a compliance report, a dashboard tooltip), use Srinivasan's plain-language framing as the translation target, not a substitute for the underlying dimension — "is it here / is it right / is it the same everywhere / is it current / does it look real / is it counted once" maps one-to-one onto completeness/accuracy/consistency/timeliness/validity/uniqueness and should be documented as an explicit translation table, not reinvented ad hoc per report.
- Add a persona attribute (or a worked-example note) to `user-persona` capturing each persona's trust posture toward automated decisions — does this persona need to see and verify the reasoning before acting on a finding, or will they accept the product's output on faith once it has proven reliable a few times? This directly extends the skill's own "Actionability" quality criterion (every attribute must be usable to write or validate a requirement) without inventing a new artifact.
- When a future governance artifact (the `data-governance-charter` DAMA-DMBOK's file already proposed) documents override/reversal rights for a Data Steward, state explicitly *why* the right exists — not just "for auditability," per `data-classification`'s current framing, but "because a steward who cannot correct a wrong automated decision stops trusting the system and starts working around it, which defeats the reason the pipeline exists."
- Before treating any future MDM adoption concern as solved by "Shafi is the sole approver, so there's no adoption problem" (the reasoning `research/data-governance-and-mdm/multi-domain-mdm-cervo-allen.md`'s Caveats section correctly used to reject an internal change-management skill), check whether the adoption question is actually about the product's *customers* trusting its automated output about their own data — a genuinely different audience from the internal, single-operator governance context that caveat was scoped to.

## Candidate New Skills/Agents

- **`data-decision-explainability` (Skill, owned by `data-architect` or `ux-architect`, consumed by `frontend-engineer` wherever a classification, match/merge, or quality-gate outcome surfaces in a customer-facing UI or report).** Teaches how to translate a specific automated data decision — this asset is Restricted, these two records were merged, this finding is at 74% confidence — into a plain-language explanation a non-technical stakeholder (per `user-persona`'s Maya Chen: technical literacy 3/5) can read, understand, and if needed dispute, without exposing raw mechanism (`entity_type`, `score`, `propagation_rule`) as the primary communication. Distinct from `data-storytelling` (an internal, retrospective trend narrative to Shafi) and distinct from `compliance-verification`/`reporting-spec` (the underlying compliance content itself, not the trust layer wrapped around a specific automated decision). Justification: this repo's entire product premise is asking customers to trust an automated estate map over their own team's folklore — every classification and match/merge decision `data-classification` and `canonical-data-model` produce is exactly the kind of decision Srinivasan's book says fails to get adopted when it isn't explainable, and no skill in the repo currently owns making one legible to the person who has to act on it.
- **`minimum-viable-mdm-scoping` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`; flag a possible merge into Loshin's already-proposed `mdm-architecture-style-selection` for whichever chunk takes this on, since both are about deliberate MDM decisions this repo currently makes by default rather than by design).** Teaches Srinivasan's lean-MDM sequencing discipline: identify the single highest-pain master entity, prove its Golden Record is trusted and adopted before extending scope, and treat "all master entities built together" as a default to question rather than a natural starting shape. Justification: `canonical-data-model` introduced `Person`, `DataSource`, and `Organisation` together in one section from the start, with no artifact anywhere asking whether that was the right sequence for a small team's first product — a genuinely different question from Loshin's per-entity architecture-style choice or Cervo & Allen's shared-infrastructure decision, neither of which asks "should we even be building this entity's MDM support yet."
- **Not proposed as a new skill, with reasoning: a dedicated MDM change-management or adoption-communications skill for this repo's own internal process.** `research/data-governance-and-mdm/multi-domain-mdm-cervo-allen.md`'s Caveats section already correctly rejected this for the *internal*, single-operator governance context — Shafi's approve-every-chunk model already is the adoption mechanism, and there is no competing internal team to win over. This book's genuinely new angle is not that rejection being wrong; it's that the same adoption-and-trust concern re-appears for a completely different audience — the product's own *customers*, who must trust automated decisions about their data — which is what `data-decision-explainability` above is scoped to, not a resurrection of the internal change-management candidate.

## Key Terminology

Candidates for `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` — cross-reference the three prior files in this cluster first: **Master Data**, **Reference Data**, **Golden Record**, **Survivorship**, **Identity Resolution**, **MDM Architectural Style**, **System of Entry**, **Hierarchy Management**, **Party Model**, **Multi-Domain MDM**, **Data Domain**, **Data Domain Owner**, **Business Data Steward**, **Technical Data Steward / Data Custodian**, **Data Governance Council**, **Cross-Domain Relationship**, and **MDM Program Maturity** are already proposed there and should not be redefined here, only cited:

- **Decision Explainability** — the practice of presenting the reasoning behind a specific automated data decision (a classification, a match/merge, a quality-gate outcome) in plain, non-technical language, so the person affected by it can understand and, if warranted, challenge it — distinct from an audit trail, which records that a decision was made and by what mechanism, but not whether the affected person can understand why.
- **Data Trust** — the confidence a non-technical stakeholder places in a data asset or automated decision, sufficient to act on it without independently re-verifying it against the source. The practical measure of whether an MDM effort has succeeded from a people perspective, independent of whether its matching/survivorship logic is technically correct.
- **Minimum Viable MDM (Lean MDM)** — Srinivasan's pragmatic starting-point discipline: solve one real, high-pain data problem for a single master domain first, prove that its Golden Record is trusted and adopted, and only then expand scope — as distinct from designing and building a comprehensive multi-entity or multi-domain program upfront.
- **Adoption Barrier (Trust-Based)** — a reason a stakeholder disengages from or works around a data system specifically because they do not understand or believe its automated reasoning, as distinct from a usability-based adoption barrier (friction, missing features, slow time-to-value) — the two require different fixes, and `user-persona`'s current attribute set only captures the latter.

## Caveats

- **This is a shorter, less formally cited, practitioner-style book than DAMA-DMBOK, Loshin, or Cervo & Allen — treat its contribution as a genuine, distinct angle (people/trust/lean-sequencing) layered on top of the other three files' mechanism-heavy coverage, not as an equally rigorous, independently citable framework with named chapters and a stable academic structure.** Where the other three files could cite specific named techniques (Fellegi-Sunter, the DAMA Wheel, the four MDM architectural styles) with reasonable confidence in their exact structure, this file's claims are grounded in the book's well-known, broadly reported thesis (plain-language MDM for business people, adoption/trust as the central risk, lean starting points for smaller programs) rather than a verified chapter-by-chapter reading. Treat the Core Concepts above as a faithful synthesis of the book's actual angle, not as page-cited fact.
- **Do not let `data-decision-explainability` become a second, competing narrative skill alongside `data-storytelling`.** The boundary drawn above (a specific automated decision explained to an external, non-technical customer at the moment they encounter it, versus a retrospective analytical trend explained to Shafi internally) must be kept explicit in whichever skill is eventually built — collapsing the two would either strip `data-storytelling`'s integrity-checklist rigor (truncated axes, cherry-picked windows) from a use case that also needs it, or bloat a decision-explainability skill with trend-narrative concerns it doesn't need.
- **The "start small, prove value" sequencing advice is directly aligned with, not a correction to, this repo's existing frugality constraint (`CLAUDE.md`'s Budget and Frugality section) and the sequencing caveats Loshin's and Cervo & Allen's own research files already gave for `hierarchy-management`, `mdm-architecture-style-selection`, and `cross-domain-relationship-modeling` ("build when a second real domain appears, not preemptively").** `minimum-viable-mdm-scoping` should be read as making that already-repeated caveat into an explicit, checkable technique rather than introducing a new philosophy — if a future chunk judges the caveat sufficient on its own, folding this candidate into `mdm-architecture-style-selection` as a single "should we build this yet" section is a reasonable alternative to a standalone skill.
- **This repo's data skills are not wrong to be mechanism-focused — that is correctly the `data-architect`'s and `data-engineer`'s job, and Srinivasan's book does not argue otherwise.** The gap this file identifies is an *missing layer* (explainability, trust, sequencing-by-proof) that should sit alongside the existing mechanism-precise skills, consumed by a different agent (`ux-architect`, `frontend-engineer`) at a different point in the pipeline (the customer-facing surface, not the pipeline internals) — not a rewrite of `data-classification`, `data-quality-rules`, or `canonical-data-model`'s existing rigor.
- **Confidence level:** I am confident in this book's headline thesis — plain-language MDM for business audiences, adoption/trust as the primary risk to an MDM program (more than matching-algorithm correctness), and a lean, prove-one-domain-first starting philosophy for smaller organizations — these are the book's well-known, consistently reported distinguishing themes relative to enterprise-scale texts like DAMA-DMBOK and Cervo & Allen. I am **markedly less confident on specific chapter structure, named frameworks, or exact terminology the book uses**, and this file does not claim page-level citation accuracy the way the DAMA-DMBOK and Loshin files attempt for their own (also unverified) chapter numbers — treat this file's grounding as thesis-level confidence, not structural confidence.
