# Multi-Domain Master Data Management: Advanced MDM and Data Governance in Practice
**Authors:** Dalton Cervo and Mark Allen
**Domain:** Data Governance and Master Data Management

## Core Concepts

- **Multi-domain MDM is a distinct architectural and organizational choice from single-domain MDM, not just "MDM applied to more entities."** Cervo & Allen's central claim is that once an organization has more than one master data domain (Customer, Product, Location/Site, Asset, Supplier, and others), the question stops being "how do we build a Customer hub" and becomes "do these domains share one governed program, one common matching/survivorship engine, and one steward structure â€” or does each domain reinvent its own." This repo's own `skills/canonical-data-model/SKILL.md` already names three master entities â€” `Person`, `DataSource`, `Organisation` â€” sharing a single skill, a single `match_decisions` table shape, and a single survivorship-rules section. That is, by accident, already a multi-domain MDM design: three domains sharing one hub mechanism. The skill never frames this as a deliberate architectural decision, though â€” it never states whether `Person`'s matching thresholds, survivorship rules, and steward-review queue are meant to be shared infrastructure that `Organisation` and `DataSource` also use, or whether each entity is expected to define its own from scratch. Cervo & Allen would treat "is this shared hub infrastructure or per-domain infrastructure" as the first architectural decision a multi-domain program must record â€” this repo has made the decision implicitly (one section, one table shape) and never named it.
- **Cross-domain relationship management â€” Customer relates to Location relates to Asset â€” is the book's signature technique, and it is a different question from anything `bounded-context-mapping` or `context-map-patterns` model.** Those two skills (both read in full) define relationships between *Bounded Contexts* â€” service-to-service integration obligations (ACL, OHS, Customer/Supplier, Published Language). Cervo & Allen's cross-domain relationship management is about relationships between *master entities themselves*, independent of which service owns which: a Customer record relates to a Location record relates to an Asset record, and that relationship graph must be modeled, stored, and kept consistent as a first-class MDM concern, not inferred from whichever services happen to call each other. This repo's actual domains have exactly this shape â€” `Organisation` presumably owns/administers a `DataSource`, a `DataSource` contains `DataAsset`s, a `DataAsset` `REFERENCES` a `Person` (per `skills/data-model-design/SKILL.md`'s Apache AGE edge list: `CONTAINS`, `REFERENCES`, `OWNED_BY`, `DERIVED_FROM`), and a `Person` may be a member of an `Organisation` â€” but nothing anywhere states this as a *master-data relationship graph distinct from the Context Map*. The `context-map-patterns` worked example's Context Map (Storage Integration â†” Classification Engine â†” Compliance Intelligence â†” Graph contexts) shows how services relate; it says nothing about how `Organisation`, `Person`, and `DataSource` as master entities relate to each other. These are two different diagrams this repo currently conflates into one (the Context Map) because only the Context Map exists.
- **A Tenant and an Organisation may or may not be the same master entity â€” and nothing in this repo says which.** `skills/multi-tenancy-design/SKILL.md`'s tenant-provisioning flow ends with "Register tenant in tenant registry service," and its Fleet Operations section speaks of "the tenant registry" as the authority on which tenants exist. `canonical-data-model` separately names `Organisation` as one of exactly three master entities with its own Golden Record, survivorship, and matching rules. Both skills are owned by different agents (`enterprise-architect` for tenancy, `data-architect` for the canonical model) and neither cross-references the other. Cervo & Allen's multi-domain discipline would force this question explicitly as step one of building a domain inventory: is "the customer's own company, extracted as an `Organisation` entity from crawled documents" the same real-world thing as "the tenant that pays for and administers this deployment," or are these two domains that merely share a word? If they are the same, the tenant registry *is* (or should be) the `Organisation` domain's system of entry; if they are different, this repo has an undocumented, unnamed fourth master domain (`Tenant`) alongside `Person`, `DataSource`, and `Organisation`.
- **A Data Governance Council with per-domain Data Domain Owners, distinct from a single undifferentiated "Data Steward," is how the book scales governance across domains.** `data-classification` (read in full) invokes "a Data Steward reviews and sets/overrides" as a single role with blanket authority over sensitivity decisions; `canonical-data-model`'s worked reasoning likewise assumes one steward can arbitrate any match/merge exception. Cervo & Allen's operating model splits this: each domain (Customer/`Person`, Product, Location, Asset, and â€” for this repo â€” `Organisation`, `DataSource`) has its own **Data Domain Owner** accountable for that domain's rules, plus a distinction between a **Business Data Steward** (defines what "correct" means for the domain â€” e.g., which source should win for `Person.display_name`) and a **Technical Data Steward / Data Custodian** (implements and enforces that rule in the pipeline). A Data Governance Council sits above all domain owners to arbitrate when two domains' rules conflict â€” e.g., `Person`'s survivorship rule escalates a record to `Restricted` because of contained PII, but the `Organisation` domain's own rules classify the containing entity differently. This repo's single-steward assumption has no mechanism for that cross-domain conflict at all.
- **An MDM program maturity model â€” assessed across governance, data quality, technology, and organizational adoption â€” is a book-native technique this repo has never applied to itself, despite the product's whole value proposition being maturity assessment of *someone else's* data governance.** A `maturity` search across every skill in the repo turns up only `skills/business-model-canvas/SKILL.md` and `skills/gtm-strategy/SKILL.md`, both product/market maturity â€” not data governance or MDM program maturity. Cervo & Allen's maturity model scores a program across dimensions such as executive sponsorship, data governance formality, data quality operating rigor, technology/architecture fit, and organizational change adoption, typically on a staged scale (ad hoc/reactive through managed, defined, and optimized). This repo has no artifact that would let it honestly answer "how mature is our own MDM program" (three master entities, one match-decisions mechanism, no documented governance council) â€” an odd absence given that `data-classification`, `compliance-design`, and the product's own positioning are entirely about assessing *customers'* data governance maturity.
- **"Domain" in this book's sense (Customer Domain, Product Domain, Location Domain) is a homonym risk against this repo's existing, precisely-defined "Subdomain" (DDD Core/Supporting/Generic classification).** `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` already defines **Domain** ("a sphere of knowledge and activity...") and **Subdomain** ("Core Domain... Supporting Subdomain... Generic Subdomain") in the strict DDD strategic-design sense used by `skills/subdomain-distillation/SKILL.md`. Cervo & Allen use "domain" to mean a master-data category (a Customer Domain, a Product Domain) â€” a completely different axis of classification that happens to reuse the same word. If a future skill introduces "Data Domain Owner" for the `Organisation` domain, a reader could easily conflate it with `subdomain-distillation`'s Core/Supporting/Generic classification of the *Party Relationship Management* subdomain (itself a candidate Core Domain by that skill's own classification checklist) â€” two unrelated meanings of "domain" stacked on each other in the same product.

## Actionable Techniques

- Before extending `canonical-data-model`'s matching/survivorship section to a fourth master entity, explicitly decide and record whether it reuses the same `match_decisions` mechanism, thresholds, and steward-review queue as `Person`/`Organisation`/`DataSource` (shared hub infrastructure) or requires its own â€” do not let the answer stay implicit the way it is today for the existing three.
- Resolve the `Tenant` (multi-tenancy-design's registry) vs. `Organisation` (canonical-data-model's master entity) question explicitly, in writing, before either skill is extended further: either state that the tenant registry is the system of entry for the `Organisation` domain (and cross-reference the two skills), or name `Tenant` as a distinct fourth master domain and design its own Golden Record treatment.
- When designing the cross-domain relationship graph (`Organisation` owns `DataSource` contains `DataAsset` references `Person`), keep it visually and conceptually separate from the Context Map â€” one shows master-entity relationships (which real-world things relate to which), the other shows Bounded-Context/service relationships (which services integrate and how). A single diagram trying to be both will misrepresent one of them.
- When a future governance artifact assigns a "Data Domain Owner" per master entity, give each domain its own row (`Person` owner, `Organisation` owner, `DataSource` owner) rather than reusing `data-classification`'s single undifferentiated "Data Steward," and name the arbitration path (a Data Governance Council, in this solo-operator context: Shafi) for when two domain owners' rules conflict.
- Split "Data Steward" into its two book-native roles wherever a future artifact needs the distinction: a Business Data Steward decision (what should the rule be â€” e.g., "source priority beats recency for `Person.primary_email`") is a different kind of judgment from a Technical Data Steward/Data Custodian decision (is the rule correctly implemented in the pipeline) â€” conflating them hides which kind of defect a stewardship failure actually is.
- Before naming anything "Domain Owner" or "Data Domain," add a one-line disambiguation next to `subdomain-distillation`'s existing DDD Subdomain classification (or in the glossary) so a reader of both skills does not conflate an MDM data domain with a DDD Core/Supporting/Generic subdomain â€” they are unrelated classifications that happen to share a word.
- If an MDM program maturity assessment is ever built, score this repo's own three-entity MDM design honestly against it first (a small, real self-audit) before offering an equivalent assessment as a customer-facing capability â€” the product's credibility in assessing customer data governance maturity is undermined if its own program has never been scored.

## Candidate New Skills/Agents

- **`cross-domain-relationship-modeling` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`, consumed by `domain-modeler` when the Apache AGE graph model is extended).** Teaches how to model relationships *between master entities* â€” `Organisation` owns `DataSource`, `DataSource` contains `DataAsset`, `DataAsset` references `Person`, `Person` is a member of `Organisation` â€” as a distinct artifact from the Context Map, using the book's Customerâ€“Locationâ€“Asset relationship-graph technique. Justification: this repo's data-model-design already has the edge types (`CONTAINS`, `REFERENCES`, `OWNED_BY`, `DERIVED_FROM`) that would carry these relationships, and the entities that need them (`Organisation`, `Person`, `DataSource`) are already named â€” but no skill treats the master-entity relationship graph as its own concern, distinct from `bounded-context-mapping`'s service-relationship Context Map, which is the only relationship diagram that exists today. Not the same as Loshin's `hierarchy-management` candidate (already proposed): hierarchy management is about same-type nesting (an `Organisation` within a parent `Organisation`); this is about cross-type relationships between different master domains.
- **`data-domain-ownership-model` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`, with conflict escalation to the Data Governance Council role â€” in this solo-operator context, Shafi).** Teaches assigning a named Data Domain Owner per master entity (`Person`, `Organisation`, `DataSource`, and any future domain), splitting "Data Steward" into Business Data Steward (defines the rule) and Technical Data Steward/Data Custodian (enforces it), and a documented arbitration procedure for when two domain owners' rules conflict (e.g., `Person`'s escalation rule vs. `Organisation`'s own classification). Justification: `data-classification` and `canonical-data-model` both currently assume one undifferentiated steward role with no domain-specific accountability and no conflict path between domains â€” a gap this book names precisely and DAMA's already-proposed `data-governance-charter` (single-authority-table scope) does not, since that candidate defines one governance structure, not a per-domain-owner structure with inter-domain arbitration.
- **`mdm-program-maturity-assessment` (Skill, owned by `enterprise-architect`, with a secondary use flagged for `product-strategist` if the assessment is ever offered as a customer-facing capability).** Teaches the book's staged maturity model (executive sponsorship, governance formality, data quality operating rigor, technology fit, organizational adoption) as a scorecard technique, usable both to self-assess this repo's own three-entity MDM design and â€” potentially â€” as a feature the product itself offers customers, given its whole positioning is assessing someone else's data governance. Justification: a repo-wide search for "maturity" surfaces only product/market-maturity content (`business-model-canvas`, `gtm-strategy`); no artifact assesses MDM or data-governance program maturity anywhere, despite this being the book's other headline technique alongside multi-domain relationship modeling.
- **`mdm-hub-shared-infrastructure-design` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`; a close but distinct companion to Loshin's already-proposed `mdm-architecture-style-selection`).** Teaches the multi-domain-specific decision of whether multiple master domains share one common matching/survivorship/steward-review mechanism (a true hub) or each builds its own â€” as distinct from Loshin's per-entity question of *which style* (Registry/Consolidation/Coexistence/Transaction Hub) a single entity uses. Justification: `canonical-data-model` already answers Loshin's per-entity question implicitly (Consolidation, for `Person`) but has never answered this book's different question â€” are `Person`, `Organisation`, and `DataSource` sharing the *same* mechanism by design, or by accident because they're described in the same skill file? As a fourth master domain is added, this decision stops being free and needs to be explicit.
- **Not proposed as a new skill, with reasoning: organizational change management for MDM adoption.** Cervo & Allen devote real space to communication plans, training, and adoption programs for getting business users to trust a Golden Record over their own local data copies. This is a genuine technique for a multi-person organization with competing data owners to win over â€” it does not map onto this repo's solo-operator, single-approver context (`CLAUDE.md`'s Shafi-approves-every-chunk model already is the adoption mechanism), and building a change-management skill here would be ceremony with no audience, directly against the frugality constraint. If a future customer-facing feature needs change-management content (e.g., helping a *customer's* organization adopt this product's compliance findings), borrow the book's framing directly in that feature's design rather than standing up a dedicated skill now.

## Key Terminology

Candidates for `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` â€” cross-reference `research/data-governance-and-mdm/dama-dmbok.md`'s and `research/data-governance-and-mdm/master-data-management-loshin.md`'s Key Terminology sections first: **Master Data**, **Reference Data**, **Golden Record**, **Survivorship**, **Identity Resolution**, **MDM Architectural Style**, **System of Entry**, **Hierarchy Management**, and **Party Model** are already proposed there and should not be redefined here, only cited:

- **Multi-Domain MDM** â€” a master data management program spanning more than one master-data domain (e.g., Customer, Product, Location, Asset) under one shared governance structure and, potentially, shared matching/survivorship infrastructure â€” as distinct from a single-domain MDM implementation covering only one entity type. Not currently named anywhere despite this repo's three master entities already sharing one mechanism in substance.
- **Data Domain** (MDM sense) â€” a named category of master data (e.g., the `Person` domain, the `Organisation` domain) with its own rules and, in a mature program, its own accountable owner. Deliberately distinguished here from the glossary's existing **Domain**/**Subdomain** entries, which describe the unrelated DDD strategic-design classification (Core/Supporting/Generic) â€” the same word, two different axes, worth a glossary note warning against conflation.
- **Data Domain Owner** â€” the individual (or, in a solo-operator context, the delegated authority) accountable for one master-data domain's rules, matching behavior, and quality â€” as distinct from a single, undifferentiated "Data Steward" spanning every domain, per `data-classification`'s current usage.
- **Business Data Steward** â€” the role that decides what a data rule *should be* for a domain (e.g., which source wins for a given attribute) â€” a judgment call, distinct from the Technical Data Steward/Data Custodian who implements and enforces that rule in code and pipelines.
- **Technical Data Steward / Data Custodian** â€” the role responsible for correctly implementing and operationally enforcing a Business Data Steward's rule â€” a different kind of failure (a bug or misconfiguration) from a Business Data Steward's failure (a wrong rule).
- **Data Governance Council** â€” the cross-domain body (or, at solo-operator scale, the single delegated authority â€” Shafi) that arbitrates when two Data Domain Owners' rules conflict, and that holds ultimate accountability for the multi-domain program as a whole, distinct from any single domain's owner.
- **Cross-Domain Relationship** â€” a modeled relationship between two master entities of *different* domains (e.g., an `Organisation` owns a `DataSource`) â€” distinct from `hierarchy-management`'s same-type nesting (an `Organisation` within a parent `Organisation`) and distinct from a Context Map's service-to-service relationship pattern.
- **MDM Program Maturity** â€” a staged assessment (typically ad hoc/reactive through managed, defined, and optimized) of an organization's master data program across governance formality, data quality rigor, technology fit, and organizational adoption â€” not present anywhere in this repo's own self-assessment, despite being central to the product's customer-facing value proposition.

## Caveats

- **Cervo & Allen write for large enterprises with genuinely competing domain owners (a Customer team, a Product team, a Supply Chain team, each with their own data and their own politics) â€” this repo has exactly one operator and no competing teams.** Every governance-operating-model recommendation above (Data Governance Council, per-domain owners, Business vs. Technical Steward split) must be scaled down the same way DAMA-DMBOK's research file already established: a documented decision-rights table Shafi approves, not a literal council with named seats for roles that don't exist yet. Building `data-domain-ownership-model` as heavyweight process for three master entities and one operator would violate `CLAUDE.md`'s frugality constraint before it delivers any value â€” the value is in explicitly naming which domain a rule belongs to and who arbitrates conflicts, not in ceremony.
- **The multi-domain relationship-modeling technique is most valuable once a genuine fourth or fifth master domain exists (an `Asset`, a `Device`, a distinct `Tenant`) â€” with exactly three entities and no confirmed conflicts between them yet, treat this as roadmap, not urgent backlog.** This mirrors the Loshin research file's own sequencing caution for `hierarchy-management` and `mdm-architecture-style-selection`: build `cross-domain-relationship-modeling` and `mdm-hub-shared-infrastructure-design` when the product's second real cross-domain question appears (e.g., "does this Organisation's `DataSource` inventory change our classification of Persons found in it"), not preemptively.
- **The book's multi-domain reference architecture assumes a dedicated MDM platform product (a commercial hub tool) sitting apart from the operational systems it serves â€” this repo's architecture deliberately has no such separate system.** `canonical-data-model`'s Golden Record lives inside the same PostgreSQL instance as the rest of the domain data, consistent with this repo's frugal, one-fewer-system-to-operate philosophy (see `data-model-design`'s Apache AGE rationale). Use this book for the *governance and relationship-modeling* vocabulary and technique, not as an argument to stand up a separate MDM platform â€” that would contradict a design choice already made and validated elsewhere in this repo.
- **The maturity model and organizational change-management chapters are the parts of this book most obviously written for a multi-team enterprise rollout â€” treat the maturity model's scoring technique as useful (a good self-assessment or customer-facing tool), but treat its underlying assumption of a dedicated MDM program office and steering committee as inapplicable at this repo's scale**, the same caution DAMA-DMBOK's research file gave for the Data Governance Council concept generally.
- **Confidence level:** I am confident in this book's headline structure â€” the multi-domain-vs-single-domain distinction, the cross-domain relationship-modeling technique (Customerâ€“Locationâ€“Asset-style), the governance operating model (Data Governance Council, per-domain owners, Business/Technical Steward split), and the staged maturity model â€” these are the book's well-established, distinguishing contributions relative to a single-domain MDM text like Loshin's. I am **less confident on exact chapter numbers and the precise labels of the maturity model's stages**, which are my best approximation of the book's structure and terminology, not verified against the physical text page-by-page.
