# DAMA-DMBOK: Data Management Body of Knowledge
**Authors:** DAMA International (DAMA-DMBOK2, 2nd Edition)
**Domain:** Data Governance and Master Data Management

## Core Concepts

- **The DAMA Wheel: Governance as the coordinating hub, not a peer discipline (Ch. 3).** DMBOK's signature diagram places Data Governance at the center of a wheel with the other ten Knowledge Areas (Data Architecture, Modeling, Storage & Operations, Security, Integration, Document & Content Management, Reference & Master Data, Data Warehousing & BI, Metadata, Data Quality) arranged around it — governance doesn't produce artifacts of its own so much as grant authority and accountability to the artifacts the other ten KAs produce. This repo has no skill occupying that hub position. `skills/data-classification/SKILL.md` leans on the hub implicitly — its propagation rules say a "Data Steward reviews and sets/overrides" and that a steward's decision is "Authoritative — wins over automated," but no skill anywhere defines who a Data Steward is, how that authority is granted, or what happens when two stewards (or a steward and an engineer) disagree. The steward is assumed, never designed.
- **Data Governance vs. Data Management: doing the right things vs. doing things right (Ch. 3).** DMBOK insists on this exact distinction — governance sets policy, decision rights, and accountability; management executes within them. Every "policy"-named skill in this repo (`skills/data-retention-policy/SKILL.md`, `skills/compliance-design/SKILL.md`) is actually a **management** deliverable: a specific, concrete rule set enacting a policy decision that was made somewhere upstream and invisibly. `CLAUDE.md`'s "Non-Negotiable Methodology" table and its hook-enforced phase gates are real governance mechanisms (they grant zero exceptions, which is what governance is supposed to do) — but nothing in the repo teaches how to *design* a governance operating model (a decision-rights/RACI table, an escalation ladder, the distinction between a durable policy and a specific standard implementing it) as its own artifact.
- **Reference Data vs. Master Data (Ch. 10).** DMBOK draws a precise line: Master Data is the core business entities referenced across many systems (Person, Organisation — exactly `skills/canonical-data-model/SKILL.md`'s subject, which handles Golden Record assembly and survivorship thoroughly and well). Reference Data is the smaller, more static controlled vocabularies and code lists used to *classify* master and transactional data — country codes, status codes, category codes. This repo has real reference data hiding in plain sight with no owner or change-control process: `data-classification`'s four-level sensitivity taxonomy (`Public/Internal/Confidential/Restricted`) and its detected-entity-type code list (`EMAIL, PHONE, SSN, HEALTH_TERM, ACCOUNT_NUMBER...`) are both reference data sets. Nothing documents who can add a fifth entity type, what a versioned change to the taxonomy requires, or how a reference-data change propagates to every ABAC policy and event schema keyed on it.
- **Metadata Management's three metadata categories (Ch. 12): business, technical, operational.** DMBOK treats metadata as a governed asset in its own right, split into business metadata (definitions, ownership, business rules — this is exactly `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md`'s job, and it's a strong, disciplined artifact), technical metadata (schemas, data types, transformation mappings, lineage structure — scattered today across `data-model-design`'s schema definitions and `data-lineage-design`'s `lineage_edges`, with no single artifact unifying "what technical metadata exists and where"), and operational metadata (job run stats, freshness, processing history — partially present in `data-pipeline-implementation`'s `processed_events`/checkpoint tables, but never once framed as *metadata to be managed and queried*, only as an implementation mechanism for idempotency). No skill unifies these three into a single metadata architecture or catalog concept.
- **Document & Content Management as its own Knowledge Area (Ch. 9) — and this product's literal subject matter.** DMBOK's Document & Content Management KA addresses the lifecycle of unstructured/semi-structured content specifically: capture, format normalization, content-level classification (as *content type*, distinct from data-sensitivity classification), version control of source documents, retrieval indexing, and content-specific retention triggers (e.g., format obsolescence risk for old `.doc` files). This repo's first product is built entirely on this KA's subject matter — scanning PDF/DOCX/XLSX across Google Drive and S3 — yet no skill treats the document as a governed content object in its own right. `data-pipeline-design` treats a file as a byte stream to extract entities from; `data-classification` treats it as a sensitivity carrier; `data-retention-policy` treats it as a disposal unit. None address document-specific concerns DMBOK calls out by name: near-duplicate document detection at the *content* level (distinct from `canonical-data-model`'s entity-level identity resolution), format-obsolescence risk, or a controlled content-type taxonomy independent of sensitivity level.
- **Data Quality as a continuous management cycle, not a one-time gate design (Ch. 13).** `skills/data-quality-rules/SKILL.md` is a genuinely strong artifact for the six dimensions, per-entity-type confidence thresholds, and three-way gate placement (pass/quarantine/reject) — but DMBOK's Data Quality KA goes further, describing an ongoing operating cycle: profile data to discover unknown quality issues, define business rules from that profiling, measure continuously, run root-cause analysis on recurring failures, and track a longitudinal DQ scorecard. `data-quality-rules` defines the gate and the DLQ-vs-quarantine split precisely, but has no root-cause-analysis step and no scorecard trending pass/quarantine/reject rates by rule over time (distinct from `data-storytelling`'s one-off narrative pattern) — a rule can silently degrade for months with no artifact designed to catch the *trend*, even though individual gate outcomes are correctly wired to `metrics-instrumentation-plan`.
- **Data Architecture as an enterprise-wide inventory, not a per-Bounded-Context model (Ch. 4).** DMBOK's Data Architecture KA operates at the level of "what data domains exist across the whole organization and how do they relate" — a cross-cutting map, distinct from any single system's schema. `data-model-design` is deliberately scoped per-service (correctly, per this repo's one-database-per-service DDD discipline), and `data-lineage-design`'s `lineage_edges` captures fine-grained derivation between specific records — but neither answers "what data domains exist across the whole product and which Bounded Contexts own which." There is no artifact playing DMBOK's enterprise-data-architecture-inventory role at all today.
- **Semantic vs. technical interoperability (Ch. 8).** DMBOK separates *technical* integration (can two systems exchange bytes — covered thoroughly by `event-schema-design` and `data-pipeline-design`) from *semantic* integration (do the two systems mean the same thing by a shared term — `canonical-data-model`'s exact job). This is a validating cross-reference more than a gap: `canonical-data-model`'s "the canonical model is a boundary artifact" discipline already embodies DMBOK's warning that solving technical interoperability alone produces what the book calls "successfully transmitted nonsense" — worth naming explicitly so future integration skills don't assume wire-format compatibility implies meaning compatibility.
- **Data Handling Ethics (Ch. 15, added in DMBOK2) — legal-but-questionable uses of data.** This KA governs uses of data that comply with law but raise ethical concern — the canonical example is capability that technically permits detailed profiling of a specific named individual even where no regulation is violated. `skills/privacy-design/SKILL.md`'s Personal Data Inventory and purpose-limitation controls are the closest existing analogue, but they are framed entirely around GDPR legal bases (Art. 6, Art. 30) — never around the separate question of whether a capability *should* exist. This product's entity-extraction and graph-traversal design (`data-model-design`'s Apache AGE `Person` vertex, queryable via `MATCH (a:DataAsset)-[:CONTAINS]->(e:Entity)-[:REFERENCES]->(p:Person)`) could technically support "find everything about person X across the estate" — a feature request `privacy-design` has no vocabulary to evaluate on ethical grounds independent of its GDPR legality.

## Actionable Techniques

- Before any future skill assumes a "Data Steward," "Data Owner," or similar named authority exists (as `data-classification` already does), write down — in whatever the eventual governance skill becomes — who holds that role for this solo-operator context (almost certainly: Shafi, or Shafi-delegated-to-an-agent-with-explicit-escalation-back-to-Shafi per `CLAUDE.md`'s existing escalation-rule pattern in `agents/data-architect.md`), rather than leaving the role as an unstated assumption threaded through multiple skills.
- Treat every enumerated code list that drives behavior (the sensitivity taxonomy, the detected-entity-type list, any future status/category enum) as **Reference Data**: give it an explicit owner, a version number, and a documented change procedure — the same rigor `event-schema-design` already requires for event wire formats, applied to the value lists those events and policies key on.
- When designing any future Metadata Management artifact, use DMBOK's three-way split (business / technical / operational) as the section headings — this avoids the common failure mode of building "a glossary" and believing metadata is now managed, when only business metadata was addressed.
- For Document & Content Management, explicitly separate "sensitivity classification" (`data-classification`'s concern: how dangerous is this content) from "content classification" (DMBOK's concern: what kind of document is this, in what format, at what version) — these are orthogonal axes today conflated into a single `sensitivity_level` field on `data_assets`.
- Add a lightweight DQ scorecard as a named artifact distinct from a one-off `data-storytelling` narrative: a small, recurring (not one-time) table of pass/quarantine/reject rate by rule and by entity type, trended over a rolling window — this is the piece of DMBOK's Data Quality cycle `data-quality-rules` stops short of.
- Before adding a "find everything about a specific named person" capability (or any capability with equivalent profiling power) to the product roadmap, run it past a data-ethics check independent of the GDPR/legal-basis check `privacy-design` already performs — legal permissibility and ethical appropriateness are different questions with different failure modes.
- When (not if) this repo's data domains grow past a handful of Bounded Contexts, build a single enterprise-wide data domain inventory (which Bounded Context owns which data domain, and which domains are Master Data vs. transactional vs. reference) before the count makes it undiscoverable by memory alone.

## Candidate New Skills/Agents

Several DAMA-DMBOK Knowledge Areas have genuinely no home in this repo's current roster — they are scattered as assumptions or side effects inside other skills rather than owned anywhere. These are proposed for a future chunk, not created now.

- **`data-governance-charter` (Skill, likely owned by `data-architect` or a new lightweight governance owner).** Teaches how to design a governance operating model scaled to a solo-operator context: a decision-rights/RACI table naming who plays "Data Steward," "Data Owner," and "escalation authority" for each data domain (today, almost certainly Shafi with delegated review to specific agents); the distinction between a durable Data Policy ("classification decisions default to automated detection unless a steward overrides") and the specific Standard implementing it (`data-classification`'s concrete rule set); and an escalation ladder for governance disputes. Justification: `data-classification` and `data-retention-policy` both assume steward/owner authority exists and is unambiguous, but this product's compliance value proposition — telling *customers* their data governance has gaps — collapses if this repo can't demonstrate its own governance structure is designed, not assumed.
- **`reference-data-management` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`).** Teaches how to govern controlled vocabularies and code lists as a distinct concern from Master Data: ownership, versioning, and change-control procedure for enumerations like the sensitivity taxonomy and the detected-entity-type list, including how a reference-data change (e.g., adding a fifth sensitivity level, or a new regulated entity type like `CRYPTO_WALLET_ADDRESS`) propagates to every ABAC policy, event schema, and UI control that keys on the old fixed set. Justification: this product's core value is precisely its taxonomy of sensitivity levels and regulated-entity types — as the product covers more regulations (HIPAA, PCI DSS mentioned as "typically out of MVP scope" in `data-classification`), these lists will need to grow, and nothing today governs that growth safely.
- **`metadata-catalog-design` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`).** Teaches DMBOK's three-category metadata model (business/technical/operational) and how to build a lightweight, queryable metadata catalog unifying what `glossary-management` (business), `data-model-design`/`event-schema-design` (technical), and `data-pipeline-implementation`'s operational tables (operational) each already produce in isolation — e.g., "which pipeline stage last touched this dataset, using which schema version, and what does the business call it." Justification: as the pipeline and schema surface grows, "what exists and what does it mean" becomes a real operational question this product's own compliance-mapping premise says every serious data organization must be able to answer about itself, not just about customers.
- **`document-content-lifecycle` (Skill, owned by `data-architect` or `data-engineer`).** Teaches Document & Content Management concerns distinct from sensitivity classification and retention disposal: a content-type taxonomy independent of sensitivity level, content-level near-duplicate detection (a customer's file server commonly holds five copies of the same contract), and format-obsolescence risk assessment (legacy `.doc`/`.xls` formats a customer's decade-old estate may still contain, and what "processed" means for a format the extraction pipeline can no longer parse reliably). Justification: this is arguably the single largest gap relative to the product's actual subject matter — the product's entire premise is mapping a document/content estate, yet no skill treats the document as a governed object with its own lifecycle concerns beyond "a file to extract entities from and eventually delete."
- **`dimensional-data-modeling` (Skill, owned by `data-architect`, consumed by `data-engineer`'s `dashboard-specification`/`reporting-spec`).** Teaches star-schema/fact-and-dimension modeling and Slowly Changing Dimension patterns for whatever analytical store eventually backs compliance dashboards and trend reports, as a companion to `data-model-design`'s explicitly OLTP/Aggregate-focused scope. Justification: `data-classification`'s worked example already shows point-in-time reclassification events (`DataAssetReclassified`) that are exactly SCD material — "what was this asset's sensitivity level as of last quarter's audit" is a real customer-facing compliance question that a normalized OLTP schema alone answers poorly.
- **`data-quality-issue-management` (Skill, owned by `data-engineer` — or folded as a major addition into `data-quality-rules` rather than standing alone; flag the merge-vs-split decision for the chunk that takes this on).** Teaches the DMBOK Data Quality operating cycle beyond gate placement: profiling to discover unknown quality problems (not just checking known rules), formal root-cause analysis when a rule's failure rate spikes, and a longitudinal DQ scorecard trended over time, distinct from `data-storytelling`'s one-off narrative pattern and from the point-in-time pass/quarantine/reject counts `data-quality-rules` already defines. Justification: `data-quality-rules`' own worked example (declining OCR confidence for scanned PDFs) is precisely the kind of *trend* a scorecard would have caught systematically rather than via one analyst noticing it.
- **`data-ethics-review` (Skill, owned by `security-architect`, consulted by `product-strategist` during Ideate/Design when a feature has profiling potential).** Teaches DMBOK's Data Handling Ethics distinction between legal permissibility and ethical appropriateness, as decision criteria (not a one-off checklist) a reasoning agent applies per feature: does this capability, though legal, enable a use (mass profiling, covert surveillance of named individuals, function creep beyond the stated compliance purpose) the product should decline to ship regardless of legality. Justification: `data-model-design`'s graph model already makes person-centric cross-estate queries technically trivial (`MATCH (a:DataAsset)-[:CONTAINS]->(e:Entity)-[:REFERENCES]->(p:Person)`), and `privacy-design` has no vocabulary for evaluating that capability's appropriateness once it's legally compliant.
- **`enterprise-data-architecture-mapping` (Skill, owned by `enterprise-architect` or `data-architect`; likely graduates into an actual reasoning task for `enterprise-architect` once there are enough Bounded Contexts to need it — flag this as a candidate to revisit once the product has more than a handful of services).** Teaches how to build and maintain a cross-cutting inventory of data domains and which Bounded Context owns each one, distinct from `data-lineage-design`'s fine-grained record-level derivation tracking and from `data-model-design`'s per-service schema scope. Justification: DMBOK's Data Architecture KA operates one level above every existing data skill in this repo — none of them currently answer "what data domains exist across the whole product," and that question gets harder to answer from memory alone as the product grows past its current single-domain MVP.

Not proposed as new skills, with reasoning: **Data Storage & Operations** (DBA operations, backup/recovery, performance tuning) is already reasonably distributed across `data-model-design`, `disaster-recovery-plan`, and `platform-engineer`'s infrastructure skills — DMBOK adds little this repo doesn't already cover, just under different names. **Data Security** is already owned thoroughly by the security cluster (`access-control-model`, `zero-trust-design`, `secrets-management`, `compliance-design`) — DMBOK's Data Security KA content substantially overlaps *Secure by Design*'s coverage already synthesized in `research/security/secure-by-design.md`, and adding a DAMA-flavored security skill would duplicate rather than fill a gap.

## Key Terminology

Candidates for `skills/glossary-management/references/ubiquitous-language.md` — none of these terms currently appear there, despite `Data Classification`, `Data Lineage`, `Data Retention Policy`, and `Data Ownership` already being present in the "Domain and DDD" and "Data Lifecycle" sections:

- **Data Governance** — the discipline of exercising authority and control over data assets: setting policy, defining decision rights, and holding roles accountable, as distinct from Data Management (executing within that policy). Currently undefined despite being implicitly invoked by `data-classification`'s "Data Steward" language.
- **Data Steward** — the accountable individual (or, in a solo-operator context, the delegated authority) responsible for the quality, definition, and appropriate use of a specific data domain, with standing authority to override automated decisions. `data-classification` uses this role extensively without ever defining it.
- **Master Data** — the core business entities referenced across many systems and contexts, requiring a single authoritative (Golden Record) version — `canonical-data-model`'s exact subject, worth naming explicitly to distinguish from Reference Data.
- **Reference Data** — controlled vocabularies and code lists used to classify or categorize master and transactional data (e.g., a sensitivity-level taxonomy, an entity-type code list) — smaller, more static, and requiring a distinct governance/versioning discipline from Master Data.
- **Metadata** — data about data: descriptive (business), structural (technical), or administrative (operational) information describing a data asset's meaning, structure, and history. Worth a precise definition before any metadata-catalog skill is written, distinguishing it from the glossary's current sole business-metadata scope.
- **Data Quality Dimension** — one of the named, independently measurable facets of trustworthiness (completeness, accuracy, consistency, timeliness, validity, uniqueness) that `data-quality-rules` already uses extensively as a term of art without it appearing in the canonical glossary.
- **Document & Content Management** — the discipline of governing unstructured/semi-structured content's lifecycle: capture, format, version, classification-as-content-type, and retrieval — distinct from data classification's sensitivity concern and data retention's disposal concern.
- **Data Handling Ethics** — the practice of evaluating whether a legally permissible use of data is nonetheless one an organization should decline, independent of and in addition to legal/regulatory compliance review.

## Caveats

- **DMBOK is written for large enterprises with dedicated governance councils, Chief Data Officers, and standing stewardship committees — none of which exist or should exist here.** Every governance concept above must be scaled down to what a solo PM operator plus Claude Code agents can actually sustain: "a documented decision-rights table Shafi approves and an agent escalates against" is the correct instantiation of DMBOK's Data Governance Council, not a literal committee with named seats. Building out `data-governance-charter` as heavyweight organizational process would directly violate `CLAUDE.md`'s frugality constraint — the value is in naming the roles and escalation paths explicitly, not in creating ceremony.
- **DMBOK's Data Architecture and Data Integration chapters assume a more centralized, enterprise-data-warehouse-era world than this repo's event-choreography-first, per-Bounded-Context design.** Where DMBOK's instinct is "a single enterprise data architecture owns all data," this repo's DDD-first `data-model-design` (one database per service, cross-context integration only via events and the canonical model) is a deliberate, already-validated architectural choice (see `research/software-architecture/fundamentals-of-software-architecture-richards-ford.md`). Use DMBOK for governance, semantics, and metadata vocabulary — not as architecture-style guidance, which is `Fundamentals of Software Architecture`'s job, already covered.
- **The Data Warehousing & Business Intelligence chapter reflects a pre-cloud-warehouse, pre-dbt era of BI tooling.** Its specific technology recommendations (traditional star-schema-in-a-dedicated-warehouse-appliance patterns) are dated; the *technique* (dimensional modeling, Slowly Changing Dimensions as a concept) remains genuinely useful and timeless, but any future `dimensional-data-modeling` skill should treat this book as a source of modeling vocabulary, not of tooling recommendations — modern cloud-warehouse/ELT practice is out of DMBOK's scope entirely.
- **Data Handling Ethics (Ch. 15) is the newest, least-established chapter in the book (added in the 2017 second edition) and has the least consensus behind it of any KA.** Treat its content as a useful prompt to ask "should we build this" rather than as an authoritative, settled framework the way the older KAs (Governance, Modeling, Quality) can be treated — DMBOK itself presents this chapter more tentatively than the rest.
- **Confidence level:** I am confident in DMBOK's headline structure — the eleven Knowledge Areas, the governance-as-hub framing, the Master/Reference Data distinction, and the three-category metadata model — these are the book's well-established, widely cited contributions. I am **less confident on exact chapter numbers**, which are my best approximation from the second edition's structure, not verified against the physical text page-by-page.
